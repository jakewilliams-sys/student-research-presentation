# Deliveroo Plus Student Strategy: Comprehensive Research Findings

*Generated: 10 March 2026 (subscription ownership data audited and corrected)*

*Data basis: Subscription-related segments across 34 of 35 participants*

---

## Executive Summary

Deliveroo Plus and Uber One have comparable subscriber penetration among students (19 Uber One, 12 Deliveroo Plus, 10 with both). The real competitive gap is not subscriber numbers but **perceived value**: students can articulate why Uber One is worth it (transport ecosystem, taxi/train value), while many Deliveroo Plus/Silver subscribers cannot explain what their subscription gives them. Free delivery is the singular dominant subscription driver (47 mentions, 21 participants) — all other benefits rank dramatically lower. Three critical barriers undermine Plus: (1) service fee erosion (27 mentions, 11 participants) makes 'free delivery' feel deceptive; (2) minimum order thresholds lock out solo student orders; (3) awareness gaps mean Prime-acquired Silver subscribers don't understand their benefits. Subscription churn is partially artificial, driven by academic calendar breaks rather than dissatisfaction, and could be reduced with a pause/resume feature.

## Competitive Landscape

### Uber One (19 current subscribers, 56% of sample)

Uber One is seen as 'worth it' by transport users regardless of food ordering frequency. The taxi/train value alone justifies the subscription cost.

**Advantages:**

- Cross-service ecosystem: taxi, train, flights create value even when not ordering food (7 participants explicitly cite this)
- Broader brand awareness through ride-hailing presence
- Cross-service cashback/credits accumulate across food + transport
- Single 'everything app' reduces switching friction

> The reason I now use Uber for essentially everything is because they have integrated with you can buy your plane tickets, trains, taxis... (P002)

### Deliveroo Plus (12 current subscribers, 35% of sample)

Deliveroo Plus is seen as a 'nice bonus' from Prime rather than a must-have subscription. Students cannot articulate what it offers beyond free delivery. *Note: Subscriber penetration is comparable to Uber One; the gap is in perceived value articulation, not reach.*

**Advantages:**

- Restaurant quality and variety perception (6 participants)
- Prime bundle makes it 'free' for existing subscribers (5 participants)
- Student Silver tier has lower barrier to entry

**Weaknesses:**

- No cross-service value beyond food delivery
- 5 participants confused about what Silver/Plus gives them
- Minimum order thresholds lock out solo ordering occasions
- Service fees undermine 'free delivery' promise

> I think this must be included with my prime or something. I don't actually entirely know what that gets me. (P002)

### Just Eat (Minimal subscription presence in sample)

Seen as cheaper for individual items but lacking subscription benefits. Service fee (£1) is a specific friction point.

> Just Eat has a service fee. Like the one euro on Just Eat and Deliveroo doesn't have that. (P032)

## Pain Points

### Service Fee Erosion [CRITICAL]

*27 mentions, 11 participants (31%)*

Students feel cheated when 'free delivery' is paired with visible service fees. The net saving feels minimal or zero.

> "They say free delivery and reduced service fees... but also sometimes even the reduced service fee is still quite a lot. (P002)"

### Minimum Order Threshold [HIGH]

*15 mentions, 8 participants (24%)*

Plus benefits are gated behind minimum order amounts (£15-25) that exceed typical solo student orders (£8-12). This makes Plus feel irrelevant for the most common ordering occasion.

> "There's a lot of restrictions with like you need to order at least £25 and it's very difficult to order over £25 if you're ordering for yourself. (P020)"

### Benefit Confusion [HIGH]

*8 mentions, 5 participants (15%)*

Students, particularly those with Silver via Prime, don't understand what their subscription provides. This reduces perceived value and weakens conversion to paid tiers.

> "Deliveroo Plus, I don't really know about, to be honest. (P022)"

### Subscription Fatigue [MEDIUM]

*10 mentions, 6 participants (18%)*

Students are increasingly conscious of subscription spending across all services (streaming, music, food, transport) and actively pruning subscriptions that don't demonstrate clear value.

> "I'm quite conscious at the moment... I'm trying to save money on subscriptions because they just leave my account so easily. (P025)"

### Overuse Guilt [MEDIUM]

*7 mentions, 4 participants (12%)*

Some students recognise that subscriptions encourage them to order more than they should, leading to health or budget guilt and eventual cancellation.

> "I would say yes. Most of the time it will be like you got a free delivery or some kind of promise on the Uber one. So that just influenced to spend more money. (P008)"

## Acquisition Channels

### Student Offer

*Conversion quality: GOOD — students who acquire through targeted offers tend to understand the proposition | 22 segments | Effectiveness: HIGH*

Student-specific offers (often via app) are the most common acquisition channel. Students actively seek student deals.

### Social Media Advertising

*Conversion quality: UNCERTAIN — high impressions but attribution to subscription sign-up is weak | 56 segments | Effectiveness: HIGH AWARENESS, UNCLEAR CONVERSION*

YouTube and TikTok ads create broad awareness but conversion path is unclear. Students recall seeing ads but don't always act.

### Freshers Fair

*Conversion quality: GOOD — captures students at peak receptivity; creates long-term association | 9 segments | Effectiveness: HIGH*

Freshers fair/week is a critical acquisition moment. Students are receptive to signing up for everything and associate it with the start of university life.

### Word Of Mouth

*Conversion quality: EXCELLENT — peer-recommended subscribers have highest engagement | 6 segments | Effectiveness: MEDIUM-HIGH*

Friends recommending subscriptions creates trust and social proof. Flatmate influence is particularly strong.

### Prime Bundle

*Conversion quality: POOR — high sign-up numbers but low benefit awareness and minimal conversion to paid tiers | 5 segments | Effectiveness: HIGH ACQUISITION, LOW ENGAGEMENT*

Prime bundle creates passive subscribers who don't actively engage with Deliveroo Silver benefits.

### Free Trial

*Conversion quality: LOW — most students cancel after trial ends unless they've reached high ordering frequency | 5 segments | Effectiveness: MEDIUM*

Free trials create temporary engagement but low post-trial conversion.

### Notification Popup

*Conversion quality: VARIABLE — depends on timing and offer quality | 4 segments | Effectiveness: MEDIUM*

In-app notifications and push notifications drive some sign-ups, particularly for free/discounted offers.

## Churn Risk Factors

### Holiday Breaks [HIGH]

Affected periods: Christmas (3-4 weeks), Easter (2-3 weeks), Summer (8-12 weeks)

**Mitigation:** Pause/resume feature

### Graduation [VERY HIGH]

Students lose student pricing and may relocate. Graduates show lowest subscription engagement (2.2 mentions/person vs 3.8 undergrad).

**Mitigation:** Graduation bridge offer with introductory rate for first 6 months post-graduation

### Subscription Fatigue [MEDIUM]

Students pruning subscriptions may cut Deliveroo first if perceived value is lower than Uber One or streaming services.

**Mitigation:** Monthly savings reports demonstrating concrete value

### Overuse Guilt [MEDIUM]

Students who recognise subscription-driven overspending may cancel as a self-control measure.

**Mitigation:** Reframe as smart budgeting tool rather than unlimited access

### Price Increase [HIGH]

Any price increase for student tiers will trigger re-evaluation; students are highly price-sensitive.

**Mitigation:** Locked-in student pricing for academic year

## Subscription Attitudes by Academic Stage

### Undergraduate (n=23)

**Engagement:** HIGH (3.8 mentions/person) | **Primary driver:** Free delivery and cost reduction

Positive when free; reluctant to pay. Evaluate ROI on per-order delivery savings. Most price-sensitive group.

- Actively use student offers and free tiers
- Compare subscription value across platforms
- Most likely to hold multiple subscriptions (free tiers)
- Highest subscription-driven ordering increase

**Plus opportunity:** Strong acquisition through free tier; conversion to paid requires demonstrating concrete savings exceed subscription cost

### Postgraduate (n=4) *(directional only — small sample)*

**Engagement:** HIGH (3.8 mentions/person) | **Primary driver:** Convenience and time-saving

More willing to pay for genuine value. Less price-sensitive but more critical of actual benefit delivery.

- Evaluate subscription on total convenience value, not just delivery fee savings
- More likely to cancel if subscription encourages unhealthy habits
- Value cross-service integration (Uber One transport + food)

**Plus opportunity:** Position Plus as time-saving tool for busy postgrads; health-forward restaurant curation could differentiate from Uber One

### Graduate (n=6) *(directional only — small sample)*

**Engagement:** MODERATE (2.2 mentions/person) | **Primary driver:** Inertia and occasional value

Less engaged with subscriptions overall. May keep Plus from habit but don't actively evaluate value.

- Lowest subscription engagement — may lapse without noticing
- Less price-sensitive so less motivated by 'savings' messaging
- More receptive to premium positioning and quality benefits

**Plus opportunity:** Transition from student to premium tier with quality-focused positioning; graduation bridge prevents lapsing

## Subscription Attitudes by Persona

### Pragmatic Optimizer

Most engaged with subscription economics. Compares platforms, calculates savings, holds multiple subscriptions strategically.

**Plus opportunity:** Show concrete savings data; these users will upgrade if the math works

> "I try to make it about £15 to save delivery fees... it makes some changes in my delivery order. (P012)"

### Social Orderer

Values subscription for reducing friction in social ordering occasions. Free delivery makes it easier to suggest group orders.

**Plus opportunity:** Position Plus as social enabler: 'Free delivery makes it easier to treat your friends'

> "So, yeah, obviously, like, I ordered from my account. Like, obviously I. I have a membership, so I ordered from my account all together and then we split it in like three or four. Like how many people are we. And then they send their share. (P009)"

### Budget Balancer

Most resistant to paid subscriptions. Will use free tiers but actively avoids paying for subscriptions they see as encouraging overspending.

**Plus opportunity:** Free tier only; do not push paid conversion. Instead, demonstrate value through occasional surprise savings.

> "I'm not really the kind of person that likes having subscriptions anyway because I just don't... like, like money coming out. (P023)"

### Convenience Dependent

Values subscription for reducing decision friction. Likely to pay if it simplifies the ordering process.

**Plus opportunity:** Position Plus as 'one less thing to think about' — automatic savings without effort

> "What made me subscribe was convenience and like, you know, some of my friends had it. (P017)"

## Strategic Recommendations

### Priority 1 -- Critical

**Value proposition simplification**

Lead all Plus messaging with 'Free delivery on every order' as singular hero benefit

*Evidence:* 47 free delivery mentions (rank 1) vs <5 for any other benefit

*Expected impact:* Improved acquisition conversion; clearer perceived value

**Service fee elimination for Plus**

Remove or absorb service fees for Plus subscribers to deliver a true 'free delivery' experience

*Evidence:* 27 service fee complaints across 11 participants; trust erosion undermines core value proposition

*Expected impact:* Increased perceived value; reduced churn from 'free delivery isn't really free' frustration

*Note: Eliminating service fees for Plus changes the PAYG experience — non-subscribers will see a more visible fee gap. This may drive conversion to Plus or feel punitive; the UX impact on non-subscribers should be tested before rollout.*

**Cross-service ecosystem**

Build strategic partnerships (transport, entertainment, lifestyle) to compete with Uber One's multi-service value

*Evidence:* 7 participants cite Uber One transport value; comparable subscriber penetration (19 Uber One, 12 Deliveroo Plus) but Uber One has stronger perceived value articulation

*Expected impact:* Reduced competitive disadvantage; new value drivers beyond food delivery


### Priority 2 -- High

**Minimum order reduction**

Reduce or eliminate minimum order requirement for Plus subscribers

*Evidence:* 15 frustration segments; solo student orders (£8-12) fall below £15-25 threshold

*Expected impact:* Unlocks Plus value for the most common ordering occasion (solo)

**Pause/resume feature**

Introduce flexible subscription pause for holiday periods

*Evidence:* Students have 3-4 non-ordering windows per year; binary stay/cancel drives unnecessary churn

*Expected impact:* Reduced churn; eliminated re-acquisition costs at predictable break points

**Silver onboarding**

Create active onboarding for Prime-acquired Silver subscribers with savings dashboard and clear upgrade path

*Evidence:* 5 participants confused about Silver benefits; passive acquisition produces disengaged subscribers

*Expected impact:* Improved Silver engagement; higher conversion to paid Plus tiers


### Priority 3 -- Medium

**Graduation bridge**

Create transition offer for graduating students with introductory paid Plus rate

*Evidence:* Graduates show lowest subscription engagement; graduation is highest churn-risk moment

*Expected impact:* Retained subscribers through life transition; lifetime value extension

**Freshers acquisition**

Double down on freshers fair/week as primary acquisition channel with compelling sign-up offer

*Evidence:* 9 freshers-related segments; critical receptivity window at start of university life

*Expected impact:* Higher initial acquisition at peak receptivity; habit formation from day one

**Peer referral program**

Build flatmate/friend referral program leveraging social influence dynamics

*Evidence:* 6 word-of-mouth segments; peer-recommended subscribers have highest engagement quality

*Expected impact:* Higher quality acquisitions through trusted social channels

## Design Questions for Experience & Design

*The strategic recommendations above raise specific UX research questions that require investigation before implementation:*

- Which app touchpoints need redesign for service fee elimination — checkout summary, order confirmation, subscription management, or savings dashboard?
- What should the Silver onboarding flow communicate to Prime-acquired subscribers who cannot articulate their current benefits?
- How does the PAYG (non-subscriber) experience change if Plus eliminates service fees? Does visible fee differentiation push PAYG users toward Plus, or does it feel punitive?
- What does the free trial journey look like end-to-end — at what point do trial users decide to cancel, and what in-app signals predict post-trial conversion?
- How should group ordering UX support the collaborative price comparison behaviour observed in social ordering contexts?

## Methodological Notes

**Data source:** Semi-structured interviews with 35 participants (25 undergraduate, 4 postgraduate, 6 graduate). 34 of 35 discussed subscription-related topics.

**Coverage:** Subscription-related coded segments across 34 of 35 participants

**Limitations:**

- Self-reported subscription ownership — no verification against actual account data
- No actual ordering frequency or spending data to validate savings claims
- Sample is UK/Ireland focused — may not generalise to other markets
- Undergraduate-dominant sample (70%) may overrepresent undergraduate subscription attitudes
- Postgraduate (n=4) and graduate (n=6) subsamples are small — stage-specific findings are indicative

**Related insights:** INS_001, INS_004, INS_005, INS_008

**Related deep dives:** DD_001, DD_004

**Diary validation note:** 15 participants completed both interviews and a 7-day diary study. Diary-tracked ordering behaviour broadly corroborates stated subscription attitudes — participants who claimed infrequent ordering showed near-zero delivery in diaries, confirming that consideration exceeds usage. However, diary data was not separately analysed for subscription-specific behaviour; a dedicated cross-tabulation would strengthen these findings.
