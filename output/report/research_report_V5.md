## Executive Summary

This research reveals fundamental misalignments between how Deliveroo approaches students and how students actually behave. Diary tracking (n=15) shows that of meals actually eaten, 60% are home-cooked on weekdays — but this drops to 43% on weekends, a 29-percentage-point collapse. Social context drives delivery behavior more than individual convenience, but current marketing focuses on personal treats rather than group enablement. The most significant finding is that delivery represents only 3.5% of actual meal occasions (diary study, n=15) despite extensive mental consideration, suggesting barriers to conversion rather than awareness gaps.

Three strategic shifts are required: First, reframe delivery as essential nutrition competing with meal skipping, not just cooking alternatives. Second, prioritize social-first features and messaging, as group dynamics drive both frequency and platform choice. Third, segment by academic stage rather than demographics - undergraduates treat delivery as emotional reward while postgraduates view it as practical utility.

The weekend opportunity is massive — home cooking drops 29 percentage points from weekdays (71% to 43% of meals eaten, diary study, n=15), creating Deliveroo's highest-value engagement window. Friday evening social planning represents the key battleground for platform choice, where collaborative price comparison and group ordering features could provide competitive advantage.

Post-graduation retention appears stronger than assumed, with graduates normalizing delivery as utility rather than treat. However, the value proposition must evolve from student perks to professional convenience. Early intervention 6 months before graduation could preserve habits through this transition.

These conclusions draw on 35 semi-structured interviews (online and in-home) triangulated with 7-day diary data from a 15-participant subsample. Three of eight insights are rated high confidence after quality audit; four were adjusted to medium confidence due to elevated contradiction rates; one (graduate behaviour, INS_005) is rated low confidence due to small sample size and is presented separately as an exploratory finding.

Integration of diary data revealed significant say-do gaps between what students claim in interviews and how they actually eat — with meal skipping, lower-than-expected cooking rates, and near-zero delivery usage during the tracked week suggesting that delivery competes with the decision to eat at all, not just with home cooking.

## Methodology

This research employed a mixed-method qualitative approach combining:

- **Online semi-structured interviews** (n=29) via Askable
- **In-home contextual interviews** (n=6) via Research Bods, including live order observation
- **7-day diary study** (n=15) via DScout — 315 meal slots across breakfast, lunch, and dinner with structured logging, daily video reflections, and open-text responses

Participants spanned three segments: undergraduate (n=25, including 6 in-home), postgraduate (n=4), and graduate (n=6). 15 participants completed both the interview and the diary study, enabling say-do gap analysis between stated attitudes and observed behaviour.

Analysis was conducted using a multi-agent system with systematic multi-pass qualitative coding, cross-participant triangulation (including interview-diary triangulation for say-do gaps), behavioural persona synthesis, and strategic insight generation, validated by quality audit and devil's advocate review.

### Limitations

- **Self-reported data**: All behavioural and attitudinal data is based on participant self-report. Ordering frequency, spend, and decision-making patterns reflect participants' own perceptions and may differ from actual usage data.
- **Sub-group sample sizes**: Postgraduate (n=4) and graduate (n=6) sub-groups are small. Findings for these segments are directional and should be validated with larger samples or behavioural data before informing strategy.
- **Platform comparison**: Competitive references (e.g., Uber One mentions) count all conversational references including non-subscribers. Like-for-like subscription comparisons are noted where applicable.
- **Diary subsample**: Statistics derived from meal-level diary data (e.g., 60% home cooking share of meals eaten, 71% weekday vs 43% weekend, 3.5% delivery share) are based on a single tracked week (January–February 2026) from the 15-participant diary subsample. These rates may vary by academic period and may not generalise to the full 35-participant sample. Cooking percentages are calculated from meals actually eaten (excluding skipped meals) to avoid deflating the rate.
- **London/UK bias**: All participants were UK-based. Findings may not generalise to other markets.

## Key Findings

### INS_001: Students skipped 22% of all meal occasions during the tracked week (diary study, n=15), with breakfast skipped 25% of the time, creating systematic disruption to eating patterns that participants discuss casually

**Why it matters:** Deliveroo competes not just with home cooking but with the decision to eat at all. This represents a larger addressable market than traditional meal replacement, but requires messaging that positions delivery as essential nutrition rather than indulgent convenience

*Evidence: 28 quotes from 35 participants (quantitative metric from diary subsample, n=15). Confidence: high.*

**Recommendations:**

- [Marketing] Develop 'fuel your day' messaging positioning delivery as essential nutrition, not treat. Test breakfast and lunch promotions targeting meal-skipping moments
- [Product] Create 'quick fuel' category with 15-minute breakfast/lunch options under £8 to compete with meal skipping


### INS_002: Of meals actually eaten, 60% are home-cooked — but this drops from 71% on weekdays to 43% on weekends (diary study, n=15), despite participants claiming consistent cooking routines in interviews

**Why it matters:** Students overestimate their cooking consistency across the week. The weekday-to-weekend collapse (a 29-percentage-point drop) reveals a massive opportunity window that Deliveroo is currently under-exploiting

*Evidence: 22 quotes from 35 participants (quantitative metric from diary subsample, n=15). Confidence: medium (QA-adjusted from high).*

**Recommendations:**

- [Marketing] Reduce guilt-inducing messaging. Position weekend delivery as normal social behavior rather than cooking failure
- [Marketing] Launch Friday-Sunday focused campaigns targeting the 29-point weekend cooking drop-off


### INS_003: Social context emerges as the strongest behavioral driver - delivery orders cluster around group situations, platform choice becomes collaborative price-comparison, and group ordering reduces individual guilt

**Why it matters:** Individual-focused marketing misses the primary driver of student delivery behavior. Group dynamics are the key to both frequency and platform choice, suggesting Deliveroo needs social-first features and messaging

*Evidence: 31 quotes from 22 participants. Confidence: high.*

**Recommendations:**

- [Product] Develop group ordering features that allow collaborative cart building and split payment before checkout
- [Marketing] Shift student marketing from individual convenience to social enablement - 'bring your friends together' rather than 'treat yourself'
- [Partnerships] Partner with student societies and halls for group ordering events and social occasions


### INS_004: Delivery apps represent only 3.5% of actual meal occasions in diary data (n=15), despite extensive interview discussion, suggesting the topic looms larger in consciousness than behavior

**Why it matters:** Students think about delivery more than they use it, indicating either barriers to conversion or overestimation of market penetration. The gap between consideration and usage represents untapped demand

*Evidence: 19 quotes from 35 participants (quantitative metric from diary subsample, n=15). Confidence: medium (QA-adjusted from high).*

**Recommendations:**

- [Marketing] Test conversion-focused campaigns targeting high-consideration, low-usage students (e.g., 3+ app opens per week with fewer than 1 order per month) with specific trigger-based messaging at browse-without-order moments
- [Product] Implement usage tracking and trigger notifications for students who browse but don't order


### INS_006: Undergraduates treat delivery as emotional reward (Friday treats, post-exam comfort) while postgraduates are more health and budget pragmatic

**Why it matters:** Student segmentation should be based on academic stage, not just demographics. Undergraduate emotional triggers require different messaging than postgraduate practical triggers

*Evidence: 24 quotes from 27 participants. Confidence: medium (QA-adjusted from high).*

**Recommendations:**

- [Marketing] Segment student campaigns by academic level: emotional reward messaging for undergraduates, practical convenience for postgraduates
- [Product] Create exam period promotions and Friday treat campaigns specifically targeting undergraduate emotional triggers


### INS_007: Friday evenings and weekend socializing drive the highest concentration of delivery orders, with social influence heavily impacting platform choice decisions

**Why it matters:** Weekend social occasions represent Deliveroo's highest-value engagement window. Winning Friday night group decisions could significantly impact market share and frequency

*Evidence: 18 quotes from 22 participants. Confidence: high.*

**Recommendations:**

- [Marketing] Concentrate marketing spend on Thursday-Friday targeting weekend social planning moments
- [Product] Launch 'Friday Night Sorted' campaign with group-friendly restaurant curation and social sharing features
- [Partnerships] Partner with student accommodation providers for Friday night group ordering events and promotions


### INS_008: Participants report sophisticated price comparison strategies across multiple platforms, with platform choice becoming collaborative decision-making in group contexts

**Why it matters:** Students are highly price-sensitive and platform-agnostic, making loyalty difficult but creating opportunities for superior group experience. Winning requires either price leadership or superior social features

*Evidence: 16 quotes from 20 participants. Confidence: medium (QA-adjusted from high).*

**Recommendations:**

- [Product] Develop transparent group price comparison tools that show Deliveroo's total cost advantage including Plus benefits
- [Marketing] Create 'Plus Calculator' showing total savings over time rather than per-order benefits to combat price comparison

## Deliveroo Plus: Student Subscription Strategy

Deliveroo Plus currently faces a significant competitive disadvantage versus Uber One among students. Uber One is referenced by 24 participants (14 current subscribers) versus 11 Deliveroo Plus subscribers, primarily because Uber One's cross-service ecosystem (taxi, train, flights) creates value beyond food delivery. Free delivery is the singular dominant subscription driver (47 mentions, 21 participants) — all other benefits rank dramatically lower. Three critical barriers undermine Plus: (1) service fee erosion (27 mentions, 11 participants) makes 'free delivery' feel deceptive; (2) minimum order thresholds lock out solo student orders; (3) awareness gaps mean Prime-acquired Silver subscribers don't understand their benefits. Subscription churn is partially artificial, driven by academic calendar breaks rather than dissatisfaction, and could be reduced with a pause/resume feature.

## Critical Review

The research findings show significant methodological strengths but suffer from critical sample bias and several unsubstantiated causal claims. 3 insights strengthened, 4 weakened, 1 unchanged. The 71% undergraduate skew creates systematic bias affecting all findings. Key blind spots include competitive dynamics, financial constraints, and seasonal variations.

- Findings strengthened: 3
- Findings weakened: 4

**8 insights challenged.**

**Findings weakened by adversarial review:**

- **INS_001:** Meal skipping may be driven by financial constraints rather than lifestyle choice, making it an unrealistic market opportunity. Students may skip meals because they can't afford delivery, not because they're choosing between delivery and nothing.
  *Recommended validation:* Segment meal-skipping analysis by financial capacity. Test whether meal skipping correlates with available spending money before positioning as market opportunity.
- **INS_002:** The cooking frequency discrepancy could reflect seasonal timing of research rather than systematic bias. Students may have been interviewed during exam periods or term-time when cooking drops naturally.
  *Recommended validation:* Validate findings across different academic periods (exam time, holidays, term start). Control for seasonal and academic calendar effects.
- **INS_004:** 3.5% usage rate may actually indicate market saturation rather than untapped demand. Students may be using delivery at their optimal frequency given budget constraints.
  *Recommended validation:* Test whether consideration correlates with available disposable income. Investigate whether barriers are financial vs. experiential before assuming conversion opportunity.
- **INS_005:** Graduate sample is too small (n=8) and potentially biased toward successful graduates who can afford increased delivery frequency. May not represent typical graduate experience.
  *Recommended validation:* Expand graduate sample significantly. Include graduates across income levels and career stages. Control for income when analyzing frequency changes.
