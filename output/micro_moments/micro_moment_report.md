# Micro-Moment Analysis: In-Home Ordering Observations

**Participants:** 6 in-home interviews (P025--P030)
**Method:** Live ordering observation during in-home visits
**Locations:** Manchester, Nottingham
**Generated:** 2026-03-06

---

## Methodology Note

This analysis is based on coded segments from in-home interviews where
participants placed real food delivery orders while being observed.
Observations carry higher analytical weight than self-reported data from
online interviews when the two conflict. Segments are classified into
ordering journey stages using research objective codes and keyword matching.

**Limitation:** In-home interviews prioritised conversational depth and contextual observation over screen-level capture of in-app interactions. As a result, mid-funnel stages (Decision Points, Final Decision) have sparser data than pre-order and post-order stages. A dedicated app-interaction observation study would be needed to fully populate these stages.

---

## Key Findings

- **Price friction is pervasive and fee-specific.** Cost concerns appeared in all 6 participants' data, though with varying intensity -- 4 of 6 raised delivery or service fees unprompted, while the remaining 2 discussed price only when prompted or in adjacent contexts. The friction is concentrated on delivery and service fees rather than food prices. Students mentally separate "the food I'm paying for" from "the extras I shouldn't have to pay," making fees feel like an unfair surcharge rather than a cost of convenience.
- **Ordering is an overwhelmingly solo activity.** Across all coded segments, solo contexts outnumber social contexts by roughly 15 to 1 (290 vs 19 segments). Note: segment counts reflect discussion volume, not ordering frequency -- solo ordering was discussed at greater length because the in-home methodology captures participants alone. The directional finding is clear, however: students order food delivery alone and eat out socially -- these are distinct behaviours with different triggers.
- **The pre-order emotional state is complex, not singular.** Comfort (32), stress (31), and excitement (29) coexist in the pre-order stage. Students do not order because they feel one thing; they order because multiple emotional states overlap -- stress from academic work, comfort-seeking as a coping mechanism, and excitement about the treat.
- **Students apply "subscription math" before committing.** Multiple participants spontaneously calculated whether their order frequency justified a monthly subscription fee, suggesting that value propositions need to be framed in terms of orders-per-month rather than absolute savings.
- **The emotional arc follows a predictable pattern.** Positive and mixed emotions dominate pre-order, the mood darkens at price exposure (stress, frustration, guilt), and anticipation returns post-order. This U-shaped emotional curve suggests price presentation is the most concentrated mood disruptor in the journey -- not the largest source of stress in absolute terms (pre-order stress is far higher in volume), but the point where negative emotions most uniformly cluster.
- **Mid-funnel behaviour is a blind spot.** Decision Points and Final Decision stages contain almost no data -- not because students skip these stages, but because the in-home methodology captured conversational context rather than screen-level app interactions. This gap limits confidence in any claims about browsing and selection behaviour.

## Price Sensitivity in Action

*How students actually react to prices vs. what they claim*

Students are not broadly price-insensitive -- they are specifically fee-sensitive. Four of six participants raised delivery or service fees without being prompted, framing them as hidden costs that undermine trust. The distinction matters: students accept paying for food they chose, but experience fees as charges imposed by the platform that do not correspond to value they can see. Several participants spontaneously performed "subscription math," calculating whether their monthly order frequency would offset a subscription fee, which reveals that students evaluate subscriptions as financial instruments rather than convenience products. This cost-benefit framing means subscription conversion depends on students believing they will order enough to break even -- a belief that shrinks as academic terms progress and ordering frequency drops.

## Social Dynamics

*How ordering changes when someone else is present*

The ratio of solo to social ordering segments is stark: 290 to 19, or roughly 15:1. This ratio reflects coded segment volume rather than discrete ordering occasions -- solo ordering generates more discussion in an in-home context where the participant is alone -- but the directional signal is clear: food delivery ordering is an overwhelmingly individual activity for these students. When social context does appear, it is tied to specific occasions -- returning from a weekend away, celebrating the end of exams, hosting friends -- rather than being a routine part of social life. Eating out and ordering in serve different social functions: students go to restaurants or takeaway shops together, but they order delivery alone. This has implications for how social proof and group-ordering features are positioned -- campaigns that frame delivery as a shared social experience may not resonate with actual behaviour, where the ordering moment is private and often emotionally driven by tiredness, stress, or self-reward.

## Emotional Arc Across the Ordering Journey

*Dominant emotions at each stage*

The emotional data reveals a U-shaped arc across the ordering journey. Pre-order is emotionally dense, with comfort, stress, and excitement appearing at nearly equal frequency (32, 31, and 29 segments respectively). This suggests students enter the ordering moment in a complex emotional state where stress and comfort coexist -- ordering food is both a response to pressure and a source of pleasure. The mid-funnel stages (App Opening, Browsing, Decision Points) are data-sparse, reflecting the methodology limitation rather than emotional absence. The critical inflection point is Price Reactions, where the mood shifts sharply negative: stress (3), frustration (3), and guilt (3) dominate in equal measure, suggesting that encountering fees is the most uniformly negative moment in the journey -- though in absolute volume, pre-order stress (31 segments) far exceeds price-reaction stress (3 segments), meaning the price stage is disruptive in character rather than scale. Post-order, the emotional tone recovers with anticipation (4) leading, but residual stress (2) persists -- likely reflecting lingering guilt about the spend.

- **Pre-Order:** comfort (32), stress (31), excitement (29)
- **App Opening:** excitement (2), relief (1)
- **Browsing:** connection (1), relief (1), deliberation (1)
- **Decision Points:** indifference (2) *(noise emotions only)*
- **Price Reactions:** stress (3), frustration (3), guilt (3)
- **Final Decision:** connection (1)
- **Post-Order:** anticipation (4), comfort (2), stress (2)

## Implications for Deliveroo

The following implications are drawn directly from patterns observed across the 6 in-home interviews. They should be treated as hypotheses warranting further validation at scale, not as confirmed strategic recommendations.

**1. Fee transparency and restructuring.** Price friction is pervasive and fee-specific. Students do not object to paying for food; they object to delivery and service fees they perceive as opaque surcharges. Restructuring fee presentation -- making fees feel earned (e.g., "your rider's fee") -- could reduce the emotional friction at the price reaction stage without changing the total cost. Bundling fees into item prices is another option, though this creates a competitive trade-off: items appear more expensive than on platforms that display lower item prices with separate fees, which could hurt conversion in a price-comparison context.

**2. Subscription value communication needs reframing.** Students spontaneously perform "subscription math," calculating whether their order frequency justifies the monthly cost. Current subscription messaging that leads with "free delivery" may be less effective than messaging that shows students their own ordering pattern and demonstrates breakeven (e.g., "You ordered 3 times last month -- Plus would have saved you £X").

**3. Optimise the solo ordering experience.** With solo ordering outweighing social ordering 15:1, the core use case is a single student ordering alone, often driven by tiredness or stress. Features and UX optimised for quick reordering, favourites, and low-friction solo checkout align better with actual behaviour than social or group-ordering features.

**4. Fill the mid-funnel research gap.** Decision Points and Final Decision stages contain almost no data due to the conversational methodology. A dedicated app-interaction observation study -- where screen behaviour is recorded alongside verbal commentary -- would be needed to understand how students actually browse, compare, and select within the app. Until this gap is filled, claims about in-app browsing behaviour rest on self-report rather than observation.
