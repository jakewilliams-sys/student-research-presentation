# Student Food Delivery Language Patterns

*Technical Reference — supporting analysis for internal use. For stakeholder-ready findings, see the Research Report.*

## Synthesis

Students frame food delivery through a **reward/guilt duality**. Positive language centres on earned indulgence — "treat" (28 uses), "reward" (8), "deserve" (4), "earned" (2) — while negative language reflects self-judgment — "lazy" (23 uses), "expensive" (4), "waste" (3). The word "treat" alone accounts for more uses than all negative terms combined, suggesting that despite guilt, the reward frame dominates.

The language also reveals a **routine vs. exception tension**. Words like "routine" (25), "habit" (9), and "ritual" (5) suggest delivery is becoming normalised, while "justified" (4) and "might as well" (6) indicate students still feel they need permission to order. Messaging that normalises delivery as routine (rather than requiring justification) could reduce this friction.

**Implication:** Marketing should lean into the "earned reward" frame — "you've earned it," "treat yourself" — rather than convenience framing. Students already use reward language naturally; amplifying it reduces guilt.
