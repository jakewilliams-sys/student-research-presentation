## The Big Picture

This research reveals fundamental misalignments between how Deliveroo approaches students and how students actually behave. Diary tracking (n=15) reveals students skip 22% of all meals and 60% of meals eaten are home-cooked (diary study, n=15), dropping from 71% on weekdays to 43% on weekends, yet Deliveroo positions itself against home cooking rather than meal skipping. Social context drives delivery behavior more than individual convenience, but current marketing focuses on personal treats rather than group enablement. The most significant finding is that delivery represents only 3.5% of actual meal occasions (diary study, n=15) despite extensive mental consideration, suggesting barriers to conversion rather than awareness gaps.

Three strategic shifts are required: First, reframe delivery as essential nutrition competing with meal skipping, not just cooking alternatives. Second, prioritize social-first features and messaging, as group dynamics drive both frequency and platform choice. Third, segment by academic stage rather than demographics - undergraduates treat delivery as emotional reward while postgraduates view it as practical utility.

The weekend opportunity is massive - cooking drops 29 percentage points from weekdays, creating Deliveroo's highest-value engagement window. Friday evening social planning represents the key battleground for platform choice, where collaborative price comparison and group ordering features could provide competitive advantage.

Post-graduation retention appears stronger than assumed, with graduates normalizing delivery as utility rather than treat. However, the value proposition must evolve from student perks to professional convenience. Early intervention 6 months before graduation could preserve habits through this transition.

## Headline Findings

### The Meal-Skipping Reality: Delivery Competes with Not Eating

Students skipped 22% of all meal occasions during the tracked week (diary study, n=15), with breakfast skipped 25% of the time, creating systematic disruption to eating patterns that participants discuss casually

Deliveroo competes not just with home cooking but with the decision to eat at all. This represents a larger addressable market than traditional meal replacement, but requires messaging that positions delivery as essential nutrition rather than indulgent convenience

> "So I don't usually eat breakfast, which is why I skipped it today. I woke up, around a normal time, like, somewhere between nine and ten. And I usually don't feel that hungry. I do usually have, like, a cup of tea and just leave it at that." — P005

> "So this morning, I I woke up late. I was a bit ill, so I didn't have time for breakfast, and I wasn't also really feeling it. I didn't feel too hungry. So I skipped breakfast, and I went straight into uni." — P007


### Ordering Together: Community and Belonging in Shared Living

Social context emerges as the strongest behavioral driver - delivery orders cluster around group situations, platform choice becomes collaborative price-comparison, and group ordering reduces individual guilt

Individual-focused marketing misses the primary driver of student delivery behavior. Group dynamics are the key to both frequency and platform choice, suggesting Deliveroo needs social-first features and messaging

> "So, yeah, obviously, like, I ordered from my account. Like, obviously I. I have a membership, so I ordered from my account all together and then we split it in like three or four. Like how many people are we. And then they send their share." — P009

> "Something about takeaways at home even with friends don't feel as like social I think because you're just eating your own thing. Whereas like if you're out and about together it feels more social for some reason." — P022


### Friday Night and Weekend Socialising: The Highest-Value Window

Friday evenings and weekend socializing drive the highest concentration of delivery orders, with social influence heavily impacting platform choice decisions

Weekend social occasions represent Deliveroo's highest-value engagement window. Winning Friday night group decisions could significantly impact market share and frequency

> "It was actually Friday night. It. I was by myself and it was a really long week and I had no food and I had no food in the fridge. I was like, it. I'm just gonna have my. My takeaway this week on Friday." — P010

> "Like, on a Friday, I guess when it's the end of the week and everyone's really tired, or, like, on a day when we don't have many groceries in the fridge, it's just easier to order food than to, like, go to the grocery store at 9 p.m." — P001


### Habit Stacking: The Gap Between What Students Say and What They Do

60% of meals eaten are home-cooked according to diary data (diary study, n=15), dropping from 71% on weekdays to 43% on weekends, despite participants claiming consistent cooking routines in interviews

Students overestimate their cooking frequency, suggesting guilt or social desirability bias around food choices. Weekend disruption creates a massive opportunity window that Deliveroo is currently under-exploiting

> "in my area there are a lot of Nepalese restaurant as well so most of the time sometimes if I'm lazy to cook at home I'll just simply pop into the restaurant or order from the ubereats Apart from that usually before to cook home cooking cook at home so it will be more healthy earlier option so I'll prefer that one" — P008

> "Weekends I'd spend a little bit more time cooking or I'd always definitely at some to have some sort of either go out somewhere to eat or get a takeaway in. Yeah, because yeah, the weeks are long and you kind of get sick of cooking." — P010


### Delivery in the Mind vs. Delivery on the Plate

Delivery apps represent only 3.5% of actual meal occasions in diary data (n=15), despite extensive interview discussion, suggesting the topic looms larger in consciousness than behavior

Students think about delivery more than they use it, indicating either barriers to conversion or overestimation of market penetration. The gap between consideration and usage represents untapped demand

*The quotes below reflect interview claims; diary tracking showed markedly lower actual delivery usage for these participants.*

> "Usually, when I'm not at home, I'm always ordering off Just Eat, Deliveroo, or UberEats, and I'm ordering to the friend's house. So about pretty much five times a week, maybe even more if I'm ordering dinner as well." — P006

> "often I tend to go for Deliveroo because Deliveroo do like A plus account for students, which in my head makes me think that I'm getting a better deal. But I tend to check delivery first and then I'll also compare that to UberEats to see which one is cheaper and then I'll order from whichever one is cheaper." — P004


### Three Distinct Student Segments, Not a Single Profile

Undergraduates treat delivery as emotional reward (Friday treats, post-exam comfort) while postgraduates are more health and budget pragmatic

Student segmentation should be based on academic stage, not just demographics. Undergraduate emotional triggers require different messaging than postgraduate practical triggers

> "I guess I think of, like, the price, the offers, and the subscription benefits all as one because it has to do with, like, cost. Right? Money, speed... I don't know if I'm as concerned. I guess, like, price is probably most important. Also, on, like, a PhD budget, it's like they're not really paying you very much." — P034

> "I had planned to cook food, but I got home late and wanted just something easy and comforting, and that was the best option." — P018


### Price Comparison as a Group Sport

Participants report sophisticated price comparison strategies across multiple platforms, with platform choice becoming collaborative decision-making in group contexts

Students are highly price-sensitive and platform-agnostic, making loyalty difficult but creating opportunities for superior group experience. Winning requires either price leadership or superior social features

> "So I've got the rewards for all, like Uber One, Just Eats... is it Plus? And Delivery Silver, I think it is. So, kind of interchange between them, looking at... depending on what deals are on and what's available." — P021

> "I would say, like: price, offers, speed, subscription, benefits... and then habit." — P036

## Priority Recommendations

### Top 5 Priorities

**1. [Marketing] Develop 'fuel your day' messaging positioning delivery as essential nutrition, not treat. Test breakfast and lunch promotions targeting meal-skipping moments** [Lifecycle, New Verticals]

**2. [Product] Develop group ordering features that allow collaborative cart building and split payment before checkout** [Quality, Plus]

**3. [Marketing] Concentrate marketing spend on Thursday-Friday targeting weekend social planning moments** [Quality, Plus]

**4. [Marketing] Reduce guilt-inducing messaging. Position weekend delivery as normal social behavior rather than cooking failure** [Affordability, Plus]

**5. [Marketing] Test conversion-focused campaigns targeting high-consideration, low-usage students (e.g., 3+ app opens per week with fewer than 1 order per month) with specific trigger-based messaging at browse-without-order moments** [Lifecycle, Affordability]


### Full Recommendation List

**1. Meal-Skipping Opportunity** [Lifecycle, New Verticals]
- Develop 'fuel your day' messaging positioning delivery as essential nutrition, not treat
- Create 'quick fuel' category with 15-minute breakfast/lunch options under £8
- Test breakfast and lunch promotions targeting meal-skipping moments
*If students are systematically skipping meals, Deliveroo can capture occasions currently lost to no eating at all*

**2. Weekend Battleground** [Affordability, Plus, Quality]
- Concentrate marketing spend on Thursday-Friday targeting weekend social planning moments
- Launch Friday-Sunday focused campaigns targeting the 29-point weekend cooking drop-off
- Reduce guilt-inducing messaging — position weekend delivery as normal social behaviour
*Weekend represents the largest behavioural shift and opportunity, with cooking dropping 29 percentage points*

**3. Social-First Features** [Quality, Plus]
- Develop group ordering features with collaborative cart building and split payment
- Shift student marketing from individual convenience to social enablement
- Partner with student societies, halls, and accommodation providers for group ordering events
*Social context drives orders more than individual need — Deliveroo needs to win the group decision-making moment*

**4. Consideration-to-Conversion Gap** [Lifecycle, Affordability]
- Test conversion-focused campaigns targeting high-consideration, low-usage students
- Implement trigger-based messaging at browse-without-order moments
*Students think about delivery more than they use it — there are barriers to overcome rather than awareness to build*

**5. Plus Value Proposition** [Affordability, Plus]
- Lead all Plus messaging with 'Free delivery on every order' as singular hero benefit
- Remove or absorb service fees for Plus subscribers
- Develop transparent group price comparison tools showing Plus benefits
*Free delivery is the only subscription driver that matters (47 mentions vs <5 for any other benefit)*

**6. Lifecycle Segmentation** [Lifecycle]
- Segment student campaigns by academic level: emotional reward for undergraduates, practical convenience for postgraduates
- Create exam period promotions and Friday treat campaigns targeting undergraduate emotional triggers
*Academic stage drives fundamentally different relationships with delivery*

**7. Graduate Retention** *(exploratory — small sample, requires further validation)*
- Investigate graduate transition messaging that repositions Plus from student perk to professional convenience
- Research whether habit preservation campaigns 6 months before graduation could reduce churn
*Early evidence (n=8) suggests graduates normalise delivery but the value proposition needs to evolve — treat this as a research question, not an action plan*
