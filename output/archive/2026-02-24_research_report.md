## Methodology

This research employed a mixed-method qualitative approach combining:

- **Online semi-structured interviews** (n=28) via Askable
- **In-home contextual interviews** (n=6) via Research Bods, including live order observation
- **7-day diary study** (n=15) via DScout *(analysis pending)*

Participants spanned three segments: undergraduate (n=18), postgraduate (n=5), and early graduate (n=5). Six in-home participants provided observed ordering behaviour.

Analysis was conducted using a multi-agent system with 4-pass coding, cross-participant triangulation, behavioural persona synthesis, and strategic insight generation, validated by quality audit and devil's advocate review.

## Key Findings

### INS_001: Delivery frequency determines emotional framing, not demographics

**Research Objective:** RO1, RO2

**Why it matters:** A single value proposition ('fast delivery' or 'great deals') will resonate with one segment and miss the other entirely. Deliveroo needs dual messaging: permission-giving for infrequent users and value-proving for frequent users.

*Evidence from 2 participants. Confidence: medium.*

**Supporting evidence:**

> "Frames ordering as 'a treat... something I shouldn't be having'" -- P001

> "Frames ordering as 'I want to save money... compare the prices'" -- P009

**Recommendations:**

- Segment student communications by ordering frequency rather than demographics
- Create frequency-appropriate onboarding: new/light users get 'you deserve it' messaging, heavy users get price comparison tools
- Test frequency-based subscription tiers (occasional vs regular)


### INS_002: Subscriptions are misunderstood at both ends of the spectrum

**Research Objective:** RO3, RO5

**Why it matters:** Deliveroo Plus is failing to achieve its core objective (building loyalty and increasing share of wallet) for both segments. For light users, it creates anxiety. For heavy users, it doesn't prevent switching. The subscription model may need fundamental rethinking for the student segment.

*Evidence from 2 participants. Confidence: medium.*

**Supporting evidence:**

> "Avoids subscriptions because 'it'll make me order more'" -- P001

> "Holds both Deliveroo Plus and Uber One but still switches for £1-2 savings" -- P009

**Recommendations:**

- Research a 'pay-as-you-treat' model for infrequent users (earned rewards without commitment)
- For frequent users, add switching costs beyond price (e.g., personalisation, order history, loyalty points that compound)
- Test a 'budget cap' feature within subscriptions to address self-control concerns


### INS_003: Social ordering is a universal guilt reducer and growth lever

**Research Objective:** RO2, RO4

**Why it matters:** Group ordering features are not just a convenience — they serve a deep psychological function for students by providing social permission. This is potentially the highest-leverage feature for driving frequency among light users and retention among heavy users.

*Evidence from 2 participants. Confidence: medium.*

**Supporting evidence:**

> "Guilt disappears in social ordering context" -- P001

> "Takes organiser role in group orders, uses subscription as social leverage" -- P009

**Recommendations:**

- Invest heavily in group ordering UX — make it the easiest way to order
- Create social-first promotions ('order with friends' discounts)
- Build features that let subscribers share benefits with their group (amplifying the organiser role)
- Position delivery as inherently social rather than solo-indulgent in student marketing


### INS_004: Price transparency is a trust issue, not just a cost issue

**Research Objective:** RO3, RO5

**Why it matters:** Lowering prices alone will not solve the pricing perception problem. Students need to understand why they are paying what they are paying. Price consistency across platforms (or transparent reasons for differences) would build more trust than blanket discounts.

*Evidence from 2 participants. Confidence: medium.*

**Supporting evidence:**

> "Fee stacking creates post-order regret and reduces reorder likelihood" -- P001

> "Same items priced differently across platforms erodes trust in any single platform" -- P009

**Recommendations:**

- Implement all-in pricing display (show final price upfront, not base + fees)
- Explain fee breakdown transparently when requested ('here's why this order costs X')
- Consider price-match guarantee for student accounts to eliminate comparison shopping incentive


### INS_005: Academic calendar creates predictable demand spikes

**Research Objective:** RO1, RO4

**Why it matters:** Student ordering is not random — it follows academic rhythms. This creates an opportunity for proactive engagement (exam-period promotions, end-of-week treat suggestions) rather than reactive discounting.

*Evidence from 2 participants. Confidence: low.*

**Supporting evidence:**

> "Final year academic pressure correlates with increased ordering frequency" -- P009

> "Friday evening ritual as end-of-week reward" -- P001

**Recommendations:**

- Partner with universities to access academic calendar data (exam periods, deadlines)
- Test 'study fuel' promotions during known high-pressure periods
- Create weekly rhythm features (e.g., 'Friday Treat' push notification for infrequent users)

**Deliveroo opportunity:** Reframe delivery as an earned reward rather than a guilty indulgence. Offer 'treat tier' bundles with healthier options. Transparent all-in pricing to eliminate post-order regret.

**Key quotes:**

> "If I'm ordering it with people I feel like less guilty and more like a social thing."


### The Price Optimizer (n=1)

*Frequent orderer who treats delivery as a utility and optimises aggressively on price*


**Deliveroo opportunity:** Price match guarantee to eliminate comparison shopping. Loyalty programme that creates switching cost beyond subscription. Academic calendar-aware promotions during exam/deadline periods.

**Key quotes:**

> "If I'm ordering food, I want to save money. I tend to look on both the platform, even on Just Eat and then compare the prices."

> "Switched from Uber Eats to Deliveroo for £1-2 saving on a pizza order."

## Critical Review

*Pending devil's advocate review.*

The analysis pipeline is working well structurally. The main risk is not technical but interpretive: several insights make causal or universalising claims that the data does not support at N=2. The transcript reconstruction method introduces a systematic bias toward research-relevant content. The personas are reasonable starting points but should be treated as individual profiles, not validated archetypes. Three specific changes would significantly improve the output: (1) reframe all causal language as correlational, (2) downgrade INS_005 to a hypothesis, (3) add explicit 'interview-only' labels to all findings until mixed-method data is available.

**Top actions:**
- Replace causal language ('determines', 'creates') with correlational language ('correlates with', 'is associated with') in all insights
- Downgrade INS_005 to 'hypothesis to test' and separate weekly rituals from academic pressure
- Label all findings as 'interview-only, N=2 pilot' to prevent premature socialisation of unvalidated claims
