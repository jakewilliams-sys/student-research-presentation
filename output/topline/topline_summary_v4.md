## The Big Picture

Student food delivery is shaped by predictable emotional and social patterns. Most students use multiple platforms, choosing between them based on occasion rather than loyalty — and Deliveroo is well-regarded for restaurant choice, quality and reliability. However, awareness and perceived differentiation of the student Plus Silver offering is lower than competitors, particularly Uber One, whose multi-benefit ecosystem makes its subscription feel broader in value. Convenience and mental effort reduction matter more than price alone: the key ordering triggers are time pressure, low energy after study or work, social occasions, and the familiar weekend treat.

## Headline Findings

### Habit Stacking: Home Habits Meet New University Behaviours

Students bring established food ordering habits with them to university and develop new ones once there. The **Friday evening 'treat' ritual** — ordering delivery as a reward for getting through the week — is a pattern most students continue from home. It was mentioned by the large majority of participants, most strongly among undergraduates and graduates.

> "If I'm gonna get a takeaway, it would probably be on a Friday night. It's not every Friday, but I'd say maybe like every other. Just kind of as, like, reward for getting through the week." — P003

Layered on top of this is a **new habit formed at university: exam period stress-driven ordering**. During exams, most students override their normal budget constraints and cooking habits, turning to delivery as a coping mechanism. This was consistent across all segments.

Together, these represent an example of habit stacking — a continued home behaviour reinforced by a new university-specific trigger — creating two predictable, high-value ordering windows.


### Ordering Together: Community and Belonging in Shared Living

Group ordering in shared accommodation is not just about splitting a bill. For many students, ordering food together is a way to **form community, build friendships, and create a sense of home away from home**. In halls and shared houses, the decision to order is often social — driven by what everyone else is doing, the desire to eat together, or a shared Friday night in.

> "At uni, I feel like because you're with friends most of the time, you kind of just do whatever everyone's doing. So whether we're eating out or cooking together or getting groceries together, that kind of happens pretty naturally." — P035

> "Each weekend there's a tradition for us to go play any sports, either football, badminton or cricket. So after that we go out to eat and for dinner mostly we cook together on weekends." — P012

These shared food moments — whether cooking or ordering — serve an emotional function beyond convenience. They help students settle, bond, and build routines together. Group ordering features that facilitate these moments have an opportunity to tap into belonging, not just basket value.


### What Students Value from Delivery

The most consistent ordering triggers across the sample were:

- **Time pressure** — late back from lectures, work, or the library
- **Low energy** — too tired to cook after a long day
- **Empty fridge** — no ingredients at home and no motivation to shop
- **Social occasions** — eating with housemates, celebrating, or a group night in
- **Weekend treat** — the familiar Friday reward ritual

Convenience and reducing mental effort matter more to students than price alone. While most students describe themselves as budget-conscious, they are willing to spend on delivery when the moment feels justified — whether that's a treat, a social occasion, or genuine time pressure.


### Deliveroo Plus Silver: Awareness and Value Clarity

Students currently receive Plus Silver free with their university email. Free delivery is the **dominant subscription value driver** — it was the most frequently mentioned benefit by a wide margin. Other benefits such as offers, cashback, and retail partners matter to some students, but most evaluate their subscription almost entirely on whether it removes the delivery fee.

However, several barriers undermine the perceived value of Silver:

- **Awareness gap** — some students don't fully understand what Silver gives them or that they even have it. Those who received it through Amazon Prime are particularly unclear on their benefits.
- **Service fee erosion** — students who expect 'free delivery' feel misled when a service fee still appears at checkout. This undermines trust in the value proposition.
- **Minimum order thresholds** — solo student orders (typically £8–12) often fall below the minimum spend required to unlock Silver benefits. Students described adding extra items purely to reach the threshold, or deciding not to order at all when the total didn't feel "worth it."

> "I think this must be included with my prime or something. I don't actually entirely know what that gets me." — P002

Subscriptions feel most worthwhile to students when paired with visible discounts, student-specific pricing, and frequent promotions. Without strong, obvious savings, students question whether the subscription is worth engaging with — even when it's free.

**Deliveroo's strengths are real.** Students consistently rated Deliveroo positively for food delivery experience, restaurant choice, and reliability. But subscription value needs to feel clearer, more student-specific, and more obviously beneficial.


### Uber One: Visibility at Key Student Moments

Uber One's multi-benefit ecosystem gives it broader perceived value among students. Beyond food delivery, students referenced using Uber for taxis, trains, and flights — making the subscription feel worthwhile even during periods of low food ordering.

But the challenge is not just about features. Uber is also **more visible at key moments in student life**. James, a 3rd-year undergraduate at Manchester Met, described a pattern familiar to many students in the sample: the Wednesday night out, the Uber home, and the seamless toggle to UberEats while still in the app.

> "UberEats is typically after a night out. I don't know if it's because I get an Uber home... oh, I see UberEats in the top corner of that, and I'm thinking, oh, that's... I forget McDonald's right now!" — P025

In these moments, Uber is visible, easy, and automatic. The food order happens inside a journey the student is already on. This kind of ecosystem visibility is difficult to replicate through food delivery alone — but it points to the importance of **being present at the right moments** rather than trying to compete on transport.


### Order Thresholds and Basket Value

There is clear evidence that minimum order thresholds and total basket requirements create friction for student users, particularly for solo orders and smaller grocery top-ups. Students frequently described checking whether an order felt "worth it" once fees were added, adding extra items purely to reach the minimum, or deciding not to order when they couldn't justify the total cost.

This friction was most pronounced for everyday or convenience-led moments. Students showed greater willingness to meet thresholds when ordering in groups, when strong discounts were present, or when the order felt like a treat or reward.

There is an opportunity to explore more student-friendly threshold mechanics — lower minimums, targeted offers, or perceived value incentives — to reduce friction on solo orders without undermining basket value.


### Three Distinct Student Segments, Not a Single Profile

Undergraduates, postgraduates, and recent graduates relate to food delivery in fundamentally different ways. Rather than a single linear progression, three distinct patterns emerged:

- **Undergraduates** combine high price sensitivity with emotional reward framing — delivery is a treat they feel they need to earn or justify.
- **Postgraduates** are the most functionally driven group — health-aware, time-poor, and the least likely to use emotional 'treat' language. They want convenience that aligns with their professional identity.
- **Graduates** unexpectedly show the strongest emotional connection to delivery. With more disposable income and less guilt, they embrace delivery as a genuine lifestyle choice rather than a budget trade-off.

These differences have implications for how Deliveroo positions its value proposition, messaging, and product features for each group.

## Priority Recommendations

**1. [Product] Launch a 'Friday Feast' feature with group ordering tools, social sharing, and premium restaurant curation specifically for Friday evenings**
Capitalises on the strongest and most consistent behavioural pattern in the data — the Friday treat ritual.

**2. [Marketing] Create Friday-specific campaigns positioning delivery as 'earned reward' rather than convenience**
Aligns with the emotional framing students already use ("reward for getting through the week") while reducing guilt.

**3. [Marketing] Develop stage-specific campaign messaging that reflects how undergraduates, postgraduates, and graduates each relate to delivery differently**
One-size-fits-all messaging misses the mark. Each segment responds to different value drivers.

**4. [Marketing] Launch targeted 'Exam Fuel' campaigns during university exam periods with comfort food curation**
Captures high-intent moments when normal budget constraints are relaxed and convenience need is acute.

**5. [Partnerships] Partner with universities to provide exam period meal deals and late-night delivery extensions during revision periods**
Positions Deliveroo as a student welfare partner while capturing stress-driven demand.

**6. [Product] Develop enhanced group ordering features with split billing, voting, and social sharing**
Reduces friction for the shared ordering moments that build community in student accommodation.

**7. [Product] Explore more student-friendly order thresholds — lower minimums or targeted incentives for solo orders**
Solo student orders typically fall below current thresholds, creating friction at the point of conversion.

**8. [Marketing] Improve Silver onboarding and value communication for students**
Many students don't know what Silver gives them. A clear savings dashboard, onboarding flow, and visible benefit tracking would increase engagement with a subscription they already have.
