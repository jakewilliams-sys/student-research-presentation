The structural disadvantage

Uber One's transport integration gives it a head start. Plus needs to find its own version of that anchor.

35% of those who discussed Uber One explicitly cite transport integration as the reason they subscribe. The food delivery is almost a side benefit — they're already paying for the taxi discounts, so the free delivery feels like a bonus. Plus currently relies on food delivery alone, which means its value is only felt at the moment of ordering. There's also an onboarding gap: students who got Silver through Prime haven't been shown what it offers, so they're subscribers who don't yet see the benefits.

35% of Uber One discussants cite transport value · 38% of Plus/Silver discussants confused about benefits · Both platforms have comparable trial rates

"The reason I now use Uber for essentially everything is because they have integrated with plane tickets, train tickets, taxis… and you get credits back on all of it."

— Rory · Also holds Deliveroo Silver via Prime

What this means for Plus

Free delivery is the foundation — but promotions and cashback are meaningful reinforcements.

Free delivery is the clear front-runner: more than twice the mentions of any other benefit, and cited by 21 of 35 participants. But promotions (12 participants) and cashback (6 participants) do register — they add to the perceived value of a subscription. The gap is at the bottom: retail partnerships and grocery offers barely exist in students' awareness, suggesting those benefits aren't landing yet.

Finding 4

Price leads, but it's not the whole story

Students are price-sensitive — but restaurant availability, habit, and switching effort quietly shape which app they actually open.

Price + friction

Price gets the comparison, but convenience often wins the order.

Students do compare prices — but the cheapest option doesn't always win. Small differences (under £2–3) rarely trigger a switch: the effort of rebuilding a cart on another app outweighs the saving. Restaurant availability is a gatekeeper — if a restaurant is only on one app, price comparison doesn't even happen. And for many students, the app they "already have open" is the one that gets the order. Nine participants hold subscriptions to multiple platforms, but default behaviour and habit still drive most individual orders.

12+ restaurant-exclusivity mentions · 8+ price-threshold mentions · 14+ habit/inertia mentions across 35 participants

"If it's only €2 of a difference, it would be whichever app I come across first. I probably wouldn't be searching for an alternative."

— Lee · Switching threshold

"I've been using Deliveroo since before uni — that's kind of what I'm used to just using to order food in general."

— Salma · Habit over price

The engagement gap

Students go dark over the holidays — and re-engaging them each term is a fresh challenge.

The student subscription is free, so the risk isn't cancellation — it's disengagement. Christmas, Easter, and summer breaks are dead zones where students go home and stop ordering entirely. Each time they return to university, the ordering habit needs to be rebuilt. The real churn risk comes at graduation, when the free subscription ends and students face a decision about whether Plus is worth paying for.

3–4 non-ordering windows per year · Graduation is the highest churn-risk moment

"I sort of bounce between all three depending on what promotions and offers are on. So I don't think I see myself ever getting a subscription for any of them just because I get good enough deals from using all three."
