Why this matters

This isn't about students lying. It's aspiration meeting reality.

Students genuinely believe they cook more and order less than they actually do. This gap between self-image and behaviour means we can't rely on what students tell us about their habits. Across 35 interviews, 110 tensions were identified between what participants claimed and how they actually behaved — spanning health, cost, routine, and identity.

The opportunity

The weekend isn't just when students stop cooking. It's when their entire routine breaks down.

Social plans override cooking intentions. Fridges empty out by Saturday. Tiredness from the week accumulates. Meal skipping nearly doubles — from 18% to 33% of all meal slots. Delivery rises from 3% to 7%, but students are more likely to default to eating out, grabbing convenience food, or simply not eating. The weekend gap is where delivery could win, but it's currently losing to inaction.

Chapter 3

It's a group decision

Social context is the strongest behavioural driver — not convenience, not hunger, not habit.

"I sat next to my mate, and he was like, 'Oh, I just think I'm going to order something.' And that's all, like, planted a seed in my head."

— Evan

1st-year undergraduate, Southampton. The trigger isn't hunger — it's social contagion.

How group ordering actually works

One friend mentions food. Then three apps open. Then one account pays for everyone.

The ordering process is deeply social. A flatmate suggests food → the group debates restaurants → someone with a subscription offers their account → one order is placed → costs are split afterwards. Platform choice happens collaboratively, with friends comparing delivery fees and promotions across apps in real time. The person with the best subscription effectively chooses the platform for the group.

The guilt factor

Group ordering doesn't just drive frequency. It reduces guilt.

Ordering alone carries guilt — students feel they "should" be cooking, saving money, eating healthier. But in a group context, delivery becomes normalised. It's a social activity, not an indulgence. The shared decision distributes responsibility, and the split cost feels more justifiable than a solo order.

What's blocking them

It's not an awareness problem. Something stops them from converting.

The emotional journey data reveals what gets in the way. When students consider ordering, the dominant emotions aren't excitement or anticipation — they're stress (169 mentions) and frustration (144 mentions). Price anxiety, guilt about spending, the mental effort of comparing platforms, and the nagging feeling that cooking is what they "should" be doing all create friction between wanting to order and actually doing it.

Chapter 5

Not all students are the same

Academic stage shapes the relationship with delivery more than age, gender, or location.

Academic stage segmentation

Undergraduates order as emotional reward. Postgraduates order for practical convenience.

For undergraduates, delivery is tied to emotion — the Friday night treat, the post-exam reward, the "I've earned it" moment. It's social, celebratory, and guilt-laden. For postgraduates, it's pragmatic — time-saving, schedule-driven, and evaluated on health and value. The same product serves fundamentally different emotional needs depending on where a student is in their academic journey.

What this means

You can't market to "students" as a single group. The triggers, barriers, and value perceptions are different.

Our research identified four distinct behavioural personas — each with different ordering triggers, emotional drivers, and pain points. But before we meet them, we need to understand the competitive landscape they're navigating: the subscriptions, the platforms, and what's actually driving platform choice.
