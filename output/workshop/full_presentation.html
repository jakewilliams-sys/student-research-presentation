The opportunity

The weekend isn't just when students stop cooking. It's when their entire routine breaks down.

Social plans override cooking intentions. Fridges empty out by Saturday. Tiredness from the week accumulates. Meal skipping nearly doubles — from 18% to 33% of all meal slots. Delivery rises from 3% to 7%, but students are more likely to default to eating out, grabbing convenience food, or simply not eating. Students consistently overestimate how much they cook — Suryansh and Mrugesh both claimed to cook 50% of their meals, but their diaries showed just 29%. The aspiration to be a home cook holds strong on weekdays, but collapses when the weekend arrives.

17-percentage-point drop in home cooking · Diary study, n=15

"It was actually Friday night. I was by myself and it was a really long week and I had no food in the fridge. I was like, I'm just gonna have my takeaway this week on Friday."

— Dearbhla · Final-year undergraduate, Dublin

"The weeks are long and you kind of get sick of cooking."

— Dearbhla

Finding 2

It's a group decision

Social context is the strongest behavioural driver — not convenience, not hunger, not habit.

How group ordering actually works

One friend mentions food. Then three apps open. Then one account pays for everyone.

The ordering process is deeply social. A flatmate suggests food → the group debates restaurants → someone with a subscription offers their account → one order is placed → costs are split afterwards. The person with the best subscription effectively chooses the platform for the group.

31 quotes from 22 participants · High confidence

"I sat next to my mate, and he was like, 'Oh, I just think I'm going to order something.' And that's all, like, planted a seed in my head."

— Evan · 1st-year undergraduate, Southampton

"I ordered from my account. I have a membership, so I ordered from my account all together and then we split it in like three or four."

— Aayush · Final-year undergraduate, London

The guilt factor

Group ordering doesn't just drive frequency. It reduces guilt.

Ordering alone carries guilt — students feel they "should" be cooking, saving money, eating healthier. But in a group context, delivery becomes normalised. It's a social activity, not an indulgence. The shared decision distributes responsibility, and the split cost feels more justifiable than a solo order.

Guilt tagged 86 times across all participants · Drops significantly in social contexts

"Oh, it's like an unspoken ritual… We're not meant to call it a 'ritual,' but it definitely is."

— Lee · 2nd-year undergraduate, Dublin

"When you're going out with friends, it doesn't feel as bad getting a pizza. It feels more like a social thing rather than ordering it to your house and eating it on your own."

— Lee

What's blocking them

It's not an awareness problem. Something stops them from converting.

Comfort and excitement pull students toward delivery — but stress, frustration, and guilt push back hard. 46% of participants had a negative emotion as their dominant response to food and ordering. Price anxiety, guilt about spending, the mental effort of comparing platforms, and the nagging feeling that cooking is what they "should" be doing all create friction. Even students who describe themselves as regular delivery users don't always follow through — Joshua W told us he gets takeaway once a week, yet recorded zero delivery orders across seven days.

Finding 4

Not all students are the same

Academic stage shapes the relationship with delivery more than age, gender, or location.

Academic stage segmentation

Undergraduates order as emotional reward. Postgraduates order for practical convenience.

For undergraduates, delivery is tied to emotion — the Friday night treat, the post-exam reward, the "I've earned it" moment. For postgraduates, it's pragmatic — time-saving, schedule-driven, and evaluated on health and value. The same product serves fundamentally different emotional needs.

24 quotes from 27 participants · Medium confidence

"Ordering a takeaway after an exam… that's an enjoyable meal. It's like a reward. You've earned it."

— Joshua W · Final-year undergraduate, Manchester

"Price is probably most important. On a PhD budget, it's like they're not really paying you very much."

— Jade · PhD student, Oxford

The structural disadvantage

Uber One's transport integration gives it a head start. Plus needs to find its own version of that anchor.

35% of those who discussed Uber One explicitly cite transport integration as the reason they subscribe. The food delivery is almost a side benefit — they're already paying for the taxi discounts, so the free delivery feels like a bonus. Plus currently relies on food delivery alone, which means its value is only felt at the moment of ordering. There's also an onboarding gap: students who got Silver through Prime haven't been shown what it offers, so they're subscribers who don't yet see the benefits.

35% of Uber One discussants cite transport value · 38% of Plus/Silver discussants confused about benefits · Both platforms have comparable trial rates

Finding 4

Price leads, but it's not the whole story

Students are price-sensitive — but restaurant availability, habit, and switching effort quietly shape which app they actually open.

Price + friction

Price gets the comparison, but convenience often wins the order.

Students do compare prices — but the cheapest option doesn't always win. Small differences (under £2–3) rarely trigger a switch: the effort of rebuilding a cart on another app outweighs the saving. Restaurant availability is a gatekeeper — if a restaurant is only on one app, price comparison doesn't even happen. And for many students, the app they "already have open" is the one that gets the order. Nine participants hold subscriptions to multiple platforms, but default behaviour and habit still drive most individual orders.

12+ restaurant-exclusivity mentions · 8+ price-threshold mentions · 14+ habit/inertia mentions across 35 participants

"If it's only €2 of a difference, it would be whichever app I come across first. I probably wouldn't be searching for an alternative."

— Lee · Switching threshold

"I've been using Deliveroo since before uni — that's kind of what I'm used to just using to order food in general."

— Salma · Habit over price

The engagement gap

Students go dark over the holidays — and re-engaging them each term is a fresh challenge.

The student subscription is free, so the risk isn't cancellation — it's disengagement. Christmas, Easter, and summer breaks are dead zones where students go home and stop ordering entirely. Each time they return to university, the ordering habit needs to be rebuilt. The real churn risk comes at graduation, when the free subscription ends and students face a decision about whether Plus is worth paying for.

3–4 non-ordering windows per year · Graduation is the highest churn-risk moment

"I sort of bounce between all three depending on what promotions and offers are on. So I don't think I see myself ever getting a subscription for any of them just because I get good enough deals from using all three."

Key takeaways

Four things to remember

  • Students want to order more than they actually do.
    The gap between consideration and conversion is real — and it's driven by friction, not lack of interest.
  • Free delivery is the foundation. Everything else builds on it.
    But it has to feel genuinely free at checkout — service fees and minimum orders are eroding the promise.
  • Ordering is social. The product should be too.
    Group decisions, flatmate influence, and Friday night planning drive more orders than individual convenience.
  • Plus has the subscribers. It needs to show them the value.
    Many students already have a subscription through Prime — they just don't know what it gives them yet.
