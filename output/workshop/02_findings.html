The opportunity

The weekend isn't just when students stop cooking. It's when their entire routine breaks down.

Social plans override cooking intentions. Fridges empty out by Saturday. Tiredness from the week accumulates. Meal skipping nearly doubles — from 18% to 33% of all meal slots. Delivery rises from 3% to 7%, but students are more likely to default to eating out, grabbing convenience food, or simply not eating. Students consistently overestimate how much they cook — Suryansh and Mrugesh both claimed to cook 50% of their meals, but their diaries showed just 29%. The aspiration to be a home cook holds strong on weekdays, but collapses when the weekend arrives.

Finding 2

It's a group decision

Social context is the strongest behavioural driver — not convenience, not hunger, not habit.

How group ordering actually works

One friend mentions food. Then three apps open. Then one account pays for everyone.

The ordering process is deeply social. A flatmate suggests food → the group debates restaurants → someone with a subscription offers their account → one order is placed → costs are split afterwards. The person with the best subscription effectively chooses the platform for the group.

31 quotes from 22 participants · High confidence

"I sat next to my mate, and he was like, 'Oh, I just think I'm going to order something.' And that's all, like, planted a seed in my head."

— Evan · 1st-year undergraduate, Southampton

"I ordered from my account. I have a membership, so I ordered from my account all together and then we split it in like three or four."

— Aayush · Final-year undergraduate, London

The guilt factor

Group ordering doesn't just drive frequency. It reduces guilt.

Ordering alone carries guilt — students feel they "should" be cooking, saving money, eating healthier. But in a group context, delivery becomes normalised. It's a social activity, not an indulgence. The shared decision distributes responsibility, and the split cost feels more justifiable than a solo order.

Guilt tagged 86 times across all participants · Drops significantly in social contexts

— Lee · 2nd-year undergraduate, Dublin

"When you're going out with friends, it doesn't feel as bad getting a pizza. It feels more like a social thing rather than ordering it to your house and eating it on your own."

— Lee

What's blocking them

It's not an awareness problem. Something stops them from converting.

Comfort and excitement pull students toward delivery — but stress, frustration, and guilt push back hard. 46% of participants had a negative emotion as their dominant response to food and ordering. Price anxiety, guilt about spending, the mental effort of comparing platforms, and the nagging feeling that cooking is what they "should" be doing all create friction. Even students who describe themselves as regular delivery users don't always follow through — Joshua W told us he gets takeaway once a week, yet recorded zero delivery orders across seven days.

Finding 4

Not all students are the same

Academic stage shapes the relationship with delivery more than age, gender, or location.

Academic stage segmentation

Undergraduates order as emotional reward. Postgraduates order for practical convenience.

For undergraduates, delivery is tied to emotion — the Friday night treat, the post-exam reward, the "I've earned it" moment. For postgraduates, it's pragmatic — time-saving, schedule-driven, and evaluated on health and value. The same product serves fundamentally different emotional needs.

24 quotes from 27 participants · Medium confidence

"Ordering a takeaway after an exam… that's an enjoyable meal. It's like a reward. You've earned it."

— Joshua W · Final-year undergraduate, Manchester

"Price is probably most important. On a PhD budget, it's like they're not really paying you very much."

— Jade · PhD student, Oxford

What this means

You can't market to "students" as a single group. The triggers, barriers, and value perceptions are different.

Our research identified four distinct behavioural personas — each with different ordering triggers, emotional drivers, and pain points. But before we meet them, we need to understand the competitive landscape they're navigating: the subscriptions, the platforms, and what's actually driving platform choice.

Now that we understand the landscape — what are they choosing between?
