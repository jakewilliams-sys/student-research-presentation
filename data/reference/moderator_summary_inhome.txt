Deliveroo Student Proposition
February 2026
Background
Deliveroo want to understand current awareness of their student proposition and how this is perceived compared to Uber One's student offering. They wanted to uncover key occasions and need states which drive delivery usage among students, as well as the customer journey and how best to optimise.
Strategic objectives
Understand how delivery brands acquire students (marketing, offers, peer influence).
Identify what drives real-time choice and loyalty.
Define what makes a student subscription feel genuinely valuable.
Explore how to retain students as they transition into graduates.
Approach
We conducted 6 x 1-hour in-home interviews with students currently studying at university
We spoke to 3 x Manchester students and 3 x Nottingham students
Within this, we included a mix of gender, grocery/food delivery usage and university years.
All students were living in university accommodation or shared houses
Life & Food Context
Student food behaviour is shaped by irregular schedules, shared living, and a lack routine rather than planned meal structures. Deliveroo should position itself as an everyday convenience solution, rather than a premium treat, aligning to how students use delivery to save time and mental effort.
Across students of different years and living situations, food behaviour is anchored in irregular schedules, multiple commitments and shared living environments, resulting in lower levels of meal planning. Long campus days, preparation for classes, part-time work and social activities mean cooking is often deprioritised in favour of quick, low effort solutions.
"When I get home from campus at 8pm I really don't feel like cooking anything." – Male, Second Year, Nottingham
Student's financial mindset is central to how they navigate food and delivery. Some describe themselves as financially conscious, particularly those receiving lower loans or limited parental support, often compensating by working additional shifts to manage living costs. At the same time, student loans can create a psychological buffer where spending doesn't feel like 'real money' before financial responsibility increases over time and into graduation.
"My Dad got a pay rise recently which means I'm just under the threshold for a higher loan. I do try and track my spending, on weeks where I've already spent a lot I'll try and limit the unnecessary costs like delivery." – Non-Binary, Third Year, Nottingham
There are also indications of a broader shift in student lifestyles, with fewer nights out or more moderated spending in other areas, potentially freeing up budget areas like delivery. However, this doesn't translate to indulgent ordering. Instead, students actively moderate their behaviour within delivery by choosing lower-cost meals, dismissing upsell costs (e.g. 'add this') and prioritising using promotions.
These 'small hacks' and mental trade-offs allow students to justify delivery while still feeling financially responsible.
"Delivery feels most worth it when there's an offer on, it kind of feels like you're winning then." – Male, First Year, Nottingham
The relationship with cooking tends to be largely functional. Meals tend to be quick and convenience led, reflecting limited time, energy and ability. Even those who enjoy cooking describe it as something they do pragmatically, noting that cooking everyday feels 'long'. As a result, delivery is not solely seen as indulgence but also as a substitute for effort-heavy cooking, especially when it provides a 'proper meal' perceived as higher quality than what they could make themselves.
"My cooking is usually quick and easy 10-minute meals, pasta or noodles. I know when I order food I am getting something I couldn't recreate myself." – Female, Second Year, Manchester
Importantly, students are not using delivery to recreate premium dining experiences. Some will actively avoid more high-end restaurants on delivery apps, seeing these as eat-in experiences rather than delivery options, reinforcing that app-based ordering sits within everyday convenience rather than an indulgent luxury.
Living arrangements can further shape this behaviour. Shared houses and student accommodation often involve disjointed schedules, limited kitchen space and parallel eating habits which directly shapes their relationship with cooking. In some cases, kitchens feel crowded, untidy, or socially awkward, reducing the desire to cook in shared spaces. Delivery therefore can become a more private, low-effort alternative, sometimes carrying an anti-social lens.
"I'm not really close with my flatmates, delivery is usually something I order alone." – Male, First Year, Nottingham
Overall, delivery within student life is rarely highly indulgent. Orders are typically moderate, value-conscious, and functional, with more 'treat-like' behaviours reserved for weekends. The perceived treat is less about luxury food and more about winning back time, reducing mental effort, and simplifying daily life within a demanding and often extreme student experience.
So what?
Student food decisions are grounded in pragmatic trade-offs between time, effort, and budget. Within this context, delivery is not positioned as a luxury splurge, but as a controlled, value-managed convenience that fits naturally into irregular routines, shared living environments, and evolving financial responsibility.
Ordering Triggers
Delivery is primarily triggered by emotional states, like stress, fatigue, low mental moments or times of celebration, rather than traditional meal occasions. Deliveroo should target these moments, positing itself as an easy pick me up and effort saving solution. Timely, value-led prompts during these moments can covert consideration into orders.
The decision to order food delivery for students is often anchored in specific emotional and contextual triggers, most commonly tied to fatigue, stress, time pressure and the need for reward or comfort.
Emotional regulation plays a significant role in triggering orders. Delivery is frequently framed as a 'pick me up' or reward, following a hard day mentally, or a great day which warrants celebration.
"If I got some results back that I wasn't happy with, I would be like 'I'm just not in the mood, I'm going to order some food." - Female, Second Year, Manchester
"If I've had a really stressful day, it feels worth it to me. I use it as a treat to lift my mood." – Non-Binary, Third Year, Manchester
There are also clear situational triggers tied to student living patterns. Weekends, hangovers, and socially busy periods increase ordering likelihood, as students prioritise relaxation and time with housemates over cooking.
"During the week, I'd usually order when I've had a really busy day so I feel like I've earned it. It's kind of the opposite on the weekend, I have more time but I normally just want to relax. I want to spend time with my housemates not be stuck in the kitchen." – Male, Second Year, Nottingham
Despite some habitual patterns, ordering is largely spontaneous; Promotions are the most influential trigger and can turn consideration into an order. They will often check deals first or become tempted after receiving discounts, with offers creating a sense of value and reduced guilt.
Delivery feels more justified when it:
Saves time after a long or busy day
Removes the effort of cooking
Provides a treat or a mental reset
Feels like good value due to promotions
Conversely, it feels less justified when food is already available at home or when the costs feel disproportionately high compared to weekly budgets.
So what?
Ordering delivery is usually an emotional response to moments in student life, e.g. tiredness, stress, a form of self care, and time scarcity. Delivery wins when it removes effort and provides an immediate reward, particularly when supported by promotions that help students justify the spend within tight and fluctuating budgets.
Live Orders: Observing Real Time Behaviours
Choice is driven by habit, subscriptions and visible value cues rather than deep brand loyalty. Deliveroo should double down on maintaining clear, always-on value through the Student proposition and prominent promotions to reinforce default app behaviour.
Most students begin the journey with a default app, shaped by familiarity and/or an active subscription, before quickly scanning for visible value cues such as promotions and deals.
Key habits observed:
Strong promotion scanning before final decision
Being nudged by offers (in-app or sent externally) can trigger spontaneous choice in the moment
Use of 'buy again' and search functions to minimise effort
Minimum spend acting as a barrier when ordering solo
Delivery time acting more as a barrier to ordering than a differentiator between apps or restaurants
Subscriptions notably shape behaviour, with students with a single subscription tend to default to that platform as it feels irrational not to use the free delivery benefits.
"I used to always open Just Eat first, that's just the one I've used the most and feel most familiar with. However, ever since I signed up to Plus I will try and only use Deliveroo, it feels stupid to use somewhere else when I have free delivery on there." – Non-Binary, Third Year, Nottingham
Those with multiple subscriptions actively compare offers and switch between apps depending on which provides the best value at the time. Free delivery across apps puts them on a level playing field in terms of value, meaning the final choice is often determined by the strength of promotions available at that moment in time.
"I build a basket on Uber and Deliveroo and compare prices. I have a subscription to both so it's usually fairly equal. Delivery fees are usually the biggest additional cost and I don't have to pay those, so it usually comes down to specific promotions available." – Male, Second Year, Nottingham
So what?
Deliveroo's student proposition is winning, with most Uber One users currently on a free trial and claiming they won't pay beyond this. Therefore, Deliveroo has a clear advantage with this group and is well positioned to hold functional loyalty throughout university periods, even among otherwise deal-driven and platform-switching students.
Grocery opportunity:
Grocery ordering is largely situational, with relevance dependent on a student's living context and proximity to a physical store. Where they don't live close to a supermarket or a small express store, rapid delivery can provide a clear functional benefit. In these scenarios, a grocery order can act as a substitute for a takeaway, used to order quick meals, ready to eat items or ingredients for an easy dinner while still retaining the convenience of not needing to leave the house.
It is seen as most justifiable in emergency missions, such as forgotten ingredients, same day meal needs or moments of low motivation to shop.
"If I realise I don't have any milk or coffee on a Sunday, I would definitely order them." – Female, Second Year, Manchester
However, for students living close to a store, grocery delivery holds limited appeal. In these cases, walking to the shop is perceived as quicker, cheaper and more rational, especially when markups, delivery fees, and minimum spend thresholds are factored in.
"We have a Sainsburys close by so I don't see why I'd ever need to order groceries. I do often see promotions for groceries, so maybe I'd consider it if it worked out a lot cheaper than going in-store but it's not something I take much notice of as I don't have a need for it." – Male, Second Year, Nottingham
Compared to food delivery, grocery ordering is framed in a far more functional and rational way. While a takeaway provides value in receiving a satisfying meal, grocery provides the exact same items available in-store, making price differences and service fees more visible and harder to justify. This can increase feelings of guilt, as students recognise they are paying a premium without gaining the same effort-saving reward as a delivered meal.
So what?
Grocery isn't competing with weekly supermarket shops, but with moments of inconvenience. Its strongest opportunity is a situational substitute for takeaways or an emergency top-up solution, particularly for students living further from shops.
Marketing & Social Discovery
There is opportunity for Deliveroo to stand out through a campus presence during Freshers week, therefore building familiarity and default app habits from the start of student's university experience.
Discovery of delivery apps is shaped more by peers and real-world visibility, rather than by traditional advertising. Some students already had apps downloaded before university (often Uber due to taxis), while others first downloaded during Freshers week. This was typically driven by introductory offers (such as money off their first X orders), rather than student-specific messaging.
"I already had Uber downloaded as I would use that at home. I think I downloaded Deliveroo when I first started because I got an offer for money off my first few orders." – Male, Third Year, Manchester
Across the board, awareness of specific marketing from delivery apps across social media is low. In general, they note that adverts they see on TikTok are often ignored or skipped, as they can feel disruptive to the experience. Instagram is perceived as a more natural and credible environment for food and offer-led content, but even here, recall of specific delivery campaigns is very limited.
Instead, the most influential discovery routes are:
Friend recommendations (particularly for subscriptions and initial downloads)
Freshers' promotions and introductory offers
On-campus presence (e.g. leaflets, codes, physical visibility)
Freshers emerged as a critical acquisition window, with early promotional codes and introductory offers acting as key triggers for downloading delivery apps. Early exposure builds familiarity that can carry throughout the university period.
Domino's was specifically cited as having a strong on-campus presence and visible offer hand-outs, whereas delivery apps were far less visible or lacked compelling offers on campus.
"Domino's were on campus handing out leaflets with special offers. Just Eat was there too but they only had QR codes to sign up, no specific offers." – Male, First Year, Nottingham
Awareness of student subscriptions and tailored benefits is also notably low unless surfaced through peers. Several students only discovered Plus through friends, and even existing users showed limited understanding of the specific benefits, suggesting current messaging is not landing and could do more to educate.
"I think I get free delivery and reduced service fees on Deliveroo, I'm not sure if I do have Deliveroo Plus though." – Male, Third Year, Nottingham
So what?
To maximise cut-through, Deliveroo should prioritise a strong on-campus presence, particularly during Freshers, supported by clear, promotion-led messaging that drives immediate trial and spontaneous consideration.
Subscription & Plus Proposition
Deliveroo's Student Plus proposition is already winning functional loyalty through cost removing benefits, but low awareness is limiting the scale of this advantage. Prioritise driving early sign-ups to Plus through high-visibility student touchpoints.
Deliveroo's student proposition has successfully created a clear but functional sense of loyalty. Students aren't emotionally attached to the brand, but they will default to the app where perks are strongest because it feels irrational to ignore free benefits.
However, awareness of the proposition is limited, there is opportunity to focus on:
Improving visibility and signups early on in student's university experience, while they are forming their default app habits.
Early touchpoints, particularly Freshers week and an on-campus presence are likely to be the most effective way to cut through to students, especially when paired with strong promotions that encourage trial and sign up.
Within a student proposition, the most appealing benefits are those that directly remove the perceived extra costs associated with delivery. Free delivery, reduced or removed service fees and lower minimum order thresholds are consistently seen as the most valuable, as they make delivery feel more justifiable and accessible for students who are often ordering alone and closely monitoring spend.
"It's the additional costs you pay to have it delivered which is best to remove, like delivery charges and services fees. Once you remove those, the orders can become pretty good value." – Male, Third Year, Nottingham
While external partnerships and broader benefits (e.g. those associated with Uber One) are acknowledged as appealing, they are largely viewed as secondary 'nice to haves'. It is the removal of visible basket costs that drives functional loyalty between apps, meaning propositions that clearly reduce upfront fees and make the total order feel like better value are most likely to stand out and win consistent usage among students.
So what?
There is opportunity to increase awareness and uptake of the existing free student proposition through early, high-visibility campus activations and offer-led acquisition, ensuring Deliveroo becomes the default app while habits are forming.
Students to Graduates Retention
The immediate post-graduation period is one of financial uncertainty, some are unlikely to convert to a paid subscription even if they value the service. Extending free or heavily discounting Plus into the 12 months post-graduation would protect habitual usage, maintain Deliveroo as the default app and bridge the gap until income stabilises.
Upon graduating, students enter a financially uncertain period. Typically, either a period of unemployment, or a graduate role, where spending becomes more scrutinised and subscriptions are among the first to be cut. The loss of student discounts across multiple categories further heightens sensitivity to ongoing subscription fees, making it unlikely that large proportions will convert to a paid Plus model immediately after graduation.
"I doubt I will pay for a subscription when I leave uni, maybe if it was a really good price and worthwhile for how much I'm ordering. I would hope I'm ordering a bit less so it's not something I want to be paying for." – Male, Third Year, Manchester
In these financially conscious years, it is unlikely that loyalty will shift from functional to emotional, instead, behaviour will continue to be driven by mental value shortcuts. If one delivery app clearly offers stronger, ongoing perks (e.g. free delivery), students will avoid switching unless a substantially better offer exists. In this sense, sustained benefit exposure can create a functional loyalty rooted in habit, familiarity and perceived smart spending.
"If I get way better benefits on one app, I would only use that and probably wouldn't bother comparing between the others because I know the price will most likely work out cheaper on that one." – Male, Second Year, Nottingham
So what?
Deliveroo can protect retention beyond graduation by extending free Plus benefits for up to 12 months or offering a heavily discounted graduate tier. This could maintain Deliveroo as the familiar go-to app and sustain habitual usage until graduates become more financially stable, where longer-term subscription payments become more viable.
