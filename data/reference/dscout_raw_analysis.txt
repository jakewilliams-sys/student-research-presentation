2. Weekday (Wed–Fri) vs Weekend (Sat–Sun)
Weekdays – Days 1–3 (Wed/Thu/Fri), 135 total meal slots:
Cooked at home: 80/135 = 59%
Skipped: 23/135 = 17%
Eating out: 14/135 = 10%
Supermarket: 9/135 = 7%
Delivery: 6/135 = 4%
Weekend – Days 4–5 (Sat/Sun), 90 total meal slots:
Cooked at home: 26/90 = 29% ↓ (halved compared to weekdays)
Skipped: 29/90 = 32% ↑ (nearly doubled)
Eating out: 13/90 = 14% ↑
Delivery: 5/90 = 6% ↑ (slightly higher)
Supermarket: 6/90 = 7% (unchanged)
Other/unclear: 11/90 = 12% — high because many participants did not log a food source for Sunday evening dinner
Key behavioural shifts between weekdays and weekends:
Home cooking drops by 30 percentage points on weekends — the single biggest finding in this comparison
Meal skipping nearly doubles; the majority of weekend skips are due to late wake-ups (participants going to bed after 6am, sleeping until afternoon), particularly Chika S and Rory C
Eating out increases slightly but is more social on weekends (with friends/family) vs. more functional on weekdays (work canteen, on-campus, café on the go)
Delivery orders are slightly more common at weekends and are more likely to be comfort/craving driven ("craving a spicebag all week", "craving pizza", "craving KFC")
Weekend eating is markedly more spontaneous and socially led; weekday eating is more habitual, budget-driven, and planned

5. Reasons for Food Choice
(Based on structured multi-select reason columns across all logged meals)Top reasons overall (estimated frequency across all non-skipped meals):
Time / convenience — most frequently cited across all meal types; especially dominant for lunch
Habit / routine — dominant for breakfast; signals automated, repeated behaviour
Price / budget — consistently cited across all meals and all participants; students are highly price-sensitive
Craving / comfort — strongest for dinner and delivery orders; most cited reason when ordering in or eating out
Socialising — very common for dinners, especially weekends; often triggers eating out
Studying or working — cited frequently at lunch as a driver of quick/convenient choices
Didn't feel like cooking — cited almost exclusively alongside delivery or eating-out choices; often paired with "craving / comfort"
Health reasons — cited most consistently by Postgrads (Jill, Louise, both mention protein goals), and by Dearbhla J, Sian W, Joshua W among undergrads
Meal prepped — a minority reason; primarily Dearbhla J (meal preps multiple portions), Joshua W (batch cooks pasta), and implicitly Rory C (subscription prep service)
By meal type:
Breakfast: Habit/routine > Time/convenience > Health reasons > Price/budget — breakfast is the most automated, least deliberate meal
Lunch: Time/convenience > Price/budget > Studying or working > Habit/routine — lunch is instrumentalised around the working/study day
Dinner: Craving/comfort > Socialising > Habit/routine > Time/convenience > Didn't feel like cooking — dinner is the most consciously chosen and socially influenced meal
Notable patterns:
"Habit/routine" almost never appears as the stated reason for a delivery order — delivery is driven by craving/comfort and convenience, not routine
"Price/budget" is cited even when eating out (e.g., Dearbhla uses Just Eat in rural Mayo because it's the only available option; Sian W goes to a restaurant specifically because of a 50% discount; Louise H orders a kids' meal at Nando's for value)
"Socialising" dramatically increases as a cited reason on Days 4–5 (Sat–Sun) — consistent with the rise in eating out and the decline in home cooking at weekends
Several participants explicitly mention TikTok as an inspiration source for what to cook at home (Louise H, Sian W mention social media for recipe ideas)
"Didn't feel like cooking" spikes when participants have had long days, are tired, or are visiting somewhere other than their usual home
6. Social Eating Patterns
General patterns by meal:
Breakfast is the most solitary meal — when eaten (rather than skipped), it is predominantly eaten alone; the exception is participants living with a partner (Jill, Louise, Chika) who occasionally share breakfast
Lunch on weekdays is predominantly solo — participants eat at their desks, in the library, or quickly between lectures; on weekends it becomes more social (eating out with friends or family)
Dinner is the most social meal — the clear majority of dinners involve at least one other person (housemate, friend, family member, or partner)
Most common social contexts:
"With housemates" — the default dinner context for university-based undergrads (Evan D, Joshua W, Sian W consistently eat dinner with housemates)
"With partner" — dominant context for Postgrads Jill L and Louise H; also Chika S and Suryansh J
"With family" — spikes on weekend days as multiple participants (Indiana K, Adam P, Dearbhla J, Sian W) were visiting or staying with family during the study period
"With friends" — strongly associated with eating-out and delivery orders; almost all restaurant meals on weekends are eaten with friends
Weekday vs weekend comparison:
On weekdays (Days 1–3): breakfast mostly alone; lunch mostly alone or with colleagues/classmates; dinner split between alone and housemates
On weekends (Days 4–5): dinner with friends or family is much more common; breakfast (when eaten at all) is more likely shared with partner; more "group" meals involving larger social gatherings
Delivery orders are more likely to be social (with partner or friends) than eating alone — ordering delivery is partly a shared experience
Individual outliers:
Rory C: almost all actual meals eaten alone — his meal prep service is designed for individual consumption; eats with friends only when someone cooks for a group (e.g., Chinese New Year dinner party)
Joshua W: highly social eater despite home cooking everything — nearly every dinner is with housemates or partner; even batch-cooks to share
Mrugesh M: most socially driven eater — consistently eats lunch and dinner with friends, often spontaneously
Edna P: highly social on weekends (all three meals with friends on Day 7); more solitary during the weekday working week
Does social eating change across the week?
Yes: eating alone is most common Mon–Fri (especially breakfast and lunch), and group eating peaks on Sat–Sun
Sunday dinner in particular is the most communal meal of the week

--- TAB: Why Ordered — Themes ---
Theme Analysis — "Why Did You Order / Choose This Meal?"
Open-text column confirmed: Breakfast/Lunch/Dinner - Why ordered (open text) — plus video transcriptions where available.
Top Recurring Themes
1. Laziness / Didn't Feel Like Cooking
The single most common delivery trigger across the dataset
"I was too lazy to cook food" (Aayush, Day 1 dinner — Uber Eats)
"We didn't feel like making late lunch/early dinner and decided to order instead" (Chika, Day 5 dinner — Deliveroo)
"Was craving KFC and didn't want to cook" (Edna, Day 5 lunch — Uber Eats)
Strongly correlates with late nights, tired days, and post-party recovery
2. Cravings — Specific Food or Cuisine
Cravings drove nearly every delivery decision, often highly specific
"We were craving pizza today" (Adam, Day 3 — Just Eat; Aayush, Day 3 — Deliveroo)
"Craving food from our cuisine (South Asian)" (Chika, Day 5)
"I was craving this dish from this specific restaurant" (Mrugesh, Day 5 — Uber Eats)
"I was craving a spicebag all week... it was in my head that we were ordering one on Sunday" (Jill, Day 5)
Craving was often pre-planned to a specific day/event, not purely impulsive
3. Time Pressure / Nothing In the House
"I was coming home late from work and fancied something quick and easy" (Louise, Day 2 — Uber Eats)
"There wasn't much food in the cupboard, I couldn't be bothered to go to the shop" (Evan, Day 2 — Uber Eats)
Running late for uni/work and skipping or grabbing on the go came up repeatedly
Katie: Greggs grabbed before a drive — "easy for all of us, quick, convenient, cheap"
4. Social Occasion / Eating with Others
Many ordering decisions were collective, not individual
"Ordered with my flatmate because we craved pizza" (Aayush)
"I am home in Mayo with the family and wanted to order a takeaway" (Dearbhla, Day 4)
Date night at Chinese restaurant (Suryansh), Chinese New Year dinner party (Rory), friend's birthday at The Real Greek (Suryansh)
Group dynamics visibly influenced both what was ordered and which platform was used
5. Price Sensitivity / Deals & Discounts
Budget was a consistent background factor, particularly for delivery app choice
"I got a deal for 2... I have Uber One so get free deliveries" (Louise, Day 2)
"We chose Deliveroo due to the free delivery" (Chika, Day 5)
"I got the best deal on Uber Eats" (Mrugesh, Day 5)
"Offer on Franco Manca" (Suryansh, Day 5)
"The restaurant has 50% off at the moment — we felt it was good value" (Sian, Day 7)
Students in particular framed eating out as a cost-justified treat when offers applied
6. Habit / Routine-Driven Choices
Many meal choices weren't decisions at all — just default behaviour
"Because Uber Eats is the app I most commonly use and am familiar with" (Evan, Day 2)
Rory used a weekly meal prep delivery service (not apps) every weekday — no deliberate decision needed
Joshua made 3 sandwiches/week as a standing routine; pasta on rotation; overnight oats nightly
Dearbhla meal-prepped regularly and described it as "not changing too much"
7. App Availability / Platform Logic
"Chose Just Eat as the other apps are not available in Mayo" (Dearbhla, Day 4) — rural delivery gap
"We remembered the restaurant was on Deliveroo" (Chika, Day 5) — prior discovery influencing platform choice
"It's the local Chinese's own app — more convenient and cheaper than another partner" (Jill, Day 5) — direct ordering to avoid commission
Uber One subscription explicitly cited as the reason for using Uber Eats over alternatives (Louise, Evan)
8. Health / Protein Goals (Implicit)
Not often cited for ordering decisions, but shaped cooking choices significantly
Dearbhla, Joshua, Louise, Sian all explicitly mentioned protein goals when explaining home-cooked meals
Health reasoning functioned as a reason not to order rather than a reason to order
Interesting / Surprising Individual Responses
Jill (Postgrad), Day 5: "I was craving a spicebag all week and it was in my head that we were ordering one on Sunday when my partner got home from the stag." — Pre-planned craving tied to a social return event. Reveals ordering as an anticipated reward/celebration, not impulse.
Chika (Undergrad), Day 5: Stayed awake until 6am, slept until 5pm, then ordered South Asian food via Deliveroo specifically citing free delivery and having seen the restaurant on the platform previously as distinct reasons. Shows how passive discovery within the app feeds into future ordering decisions.
Dearbhla (Undergrad), Day 4: Rural delivery gap — Deliveroo and Uber Eats literally not available in Mayo. Defaulted to Just Eat as the only option. Points to uneven platform penetration outside cities.
Evan (Undergrad), Day 2: "Because Uber Eats is the app I most commonly use and am familiar with" — The explicit reason for choosing a platform was app familiarity alone, not price or selection.
Rory (Undergrad): Never used a delivery app once. Subscribed to a weekly meal prep service delivered on Mondays, used throughout the week. Entirely opted out of the delivery app ecosystem.
Suryansh (Undergrad), Day 5: "Offer on Franco Manca" — Just three words, but one of the most transactionally clear responses. A deal was the primary (apparently sole) stated reason.
Early Week vs Late Week Patterns
Early week (Days 1–3, roughly Tue–Fri):
Dominated by routine, cooking at home, meal prep, budget discipline
Delivery orders rare and usually triggered by specific situational factors (flatmate, no food in house, late day)
More structured decision-making language in responses
Late week (Days 4–7, Fri–Mon, covering the weekend):
Clear spike in social eating, restaurant outings, and delivery orders
Cravings and social occasions dominate; cost discipline relaxed ("it was the weekend, treat myself")
Multiple participants noted that weekend patterns differ from their norm — more spontaneous, more indulgent
Several specifically cited a sense of permission to order on weekends: "it is the weekend, I decided to treat myself" (Edna); "at the weekend you're able to splash out a bit more" (Katie)
By Day 7 (Monday), several participants showed signs of returning to routine — Rory explicitly noted "today was a bit more back to my normal routine"

--- TAB: Video Reflection Themes ---
1. Main Themes in Reflections
Habit & Routine
Many participants describe near-automated eating patterns: same breakfast daily, fixed meal prep schedules, Thursday Starbucks runs
"It's just part of my routine now" is a phrase that recurs almost verbatim across several participants
Routine is most strongly articulated by undergrads (Evan D, Joshua W, Sian W, Aayush M)
Time Pressure & Lecture Scheduling
The most dominant driver of skipped meals: early lectures, long shifts, doctor's appointments, commutes
Breakfast is skipped most frequently; multiple students frame this as near-identity ("I'm not a breakfast person anyway")
Budget Consciousness
Cost is explicitly cited across nearly all undergrads — "cheap, easy, and it's in the cupboard" (Evan D); "very budget day" (Joshua W)
Meal prep services, leftovers, and home cooking are described in economic terms more than nutritional ones
Social Context as the Primary Weekend Override
Weekend eating is dramatically different from weekday — more eating out, more indulgence, more spontaneity
Food decisions often made reactively to social invitations rather than planned: "one of my friends called and asked if I wanted to get food and I was like, yeah okay" (Mrugesh M)
Chinese New Year, visits home, trips to Swansea/Mayo, rave events, pub sessions all appear as context-coloring food choices
Disrupted Routine / Being Away from Uni
A notable subset of participants (Indiana K, Adam P, Dearbhla J) were visiting their family home during the study, which they consistently flagged as distorting their data
"It doesn't really reflect my day to day" is the most repeated phrase in the entire dataset — used by almost every participant in almost every reflection
Comfort & Craving
Appears particularly in evenings and weekends — shakshuka as a "comfort meal," Nando's craving, McDonald's cravings, spice bag being "in my head all week" (Jill L)
Emotional eating framed as benign and deserved ("treat meal") rather than guilty
Meal Skipping as Normal
Skipping breakfast is described as habitual and identity-level, not as a lapse
Skipping lunch is usually attributed to logistics; skipping dinner is rarer and almost always explained by illness, parties, or fatigue
Health Awareness — Selective and Purposeful
A subset (Louise H, Sian W, Joshua W, Dearbhla J) actively track protein intake and make food choices framed around nutrition goals
But this sits alongside cheat meals and convenience eating without conflict — participants compartmentalize rather than moralize
2. How Reflections Evolve: Day 2 → Day 4 → Day 7
Day 2 — Descriptive, Slightly Self-Conscious
Participants describe meals in detail, explaining the "why" behind each choice
Lots of contextualizing and hedging: "this isn't typical because..." / "I was running late so..."
Tone is observational and slightly performative — participants seem aware they're being evaluated
Several voluntarily compare their behavior to their "normal," often to flag it's different this week
Day 4 — More Natural, More Social
Reflections become looser and more conversational
Weekend behaviors dominate: parties, visits, social events, cultural celebrations
Less justification of choices — participants are more matter-of-fact
Some begin to notice patterns mid-week: Evan D comments that he was "being lazy" and plans to "try and limit takeaways"
A few reflect directly on the act of logging (Mrugesh M, Rory C) rather than just on food
Day 7 — Retrospective, More Emotionally Resonant
This is the most qualitatively rich day across the dataset
Multiple participants explicitly review the whole week as a unit — "looking at this journal and this diary I've been doing" (Edna P)
Language of intention begins to appear: "my aim will be more to cook at home more" (Edna P); "we're back on breakfast, lunch, and dinner" (Katie L)
Some participants become more honest about behavior they softened earlier: Joshua W acknowledges the study week was unusually budget-heavy; Katie L admits she's been skipping meals all week
Evan D's final reflection is the starkest: short, resigned, no change — "not much changing from that, to be honest"
Rory C explicitly says this week was a better representation than last week — meta-awareness of the study itself

4. Emotional Patterns & Notable Language
"It doesn't reflect my day to day" — the most universal phrase across all participants and all three reflection days. Nearly everyone uses it or a close variant. Suggests participants feel an implicit pressure to present a normative diet and consistently excuse divergence.Apologetic hedging, mostly from undergrads:
"I know I said this before," "sorry about the background noise," "I guess"
Females slightly more likely to hedge than males in this sample
No guilt, lots of explanation:
Unlike what you might expect from a food diary study, almost no participant expresses shame or guilt
Comfort eating and skipping meals are framed as logical responses to circumstances, not character failures
Tiredness as the silent driver:
"Couldn't be bothered to cook," "wasn't bothered," "too tired," "just needed something" — fatigue underlies a huge proportion of convenience food choices but is rarely named as the primary reason in the "Reason for choice" column (which shows only coded options like "Time/convenience")
Qualitative shift in Day 7 emotional register:
Day 7 reflections are warmer, more reflective, more willing to make statements about the future self
The study appears to have prompted genuine behavioral reflection for at least Edna P, Katie L, and Louise H
For others (Evan D, Rory C), the final reflection is more neutral — the study confirmed existing behavior rather than challenging it
Cultural identity woven into food language:
Indian-background students (Aayush M, Mrugesh M, Suryansh J) naturally code-switch when describing home cooking — references to biryani, palak, chapatis, and Indian curry carry emotional warmth absent from descriptions of supermarket meal deals
Chika S explicitly names her cuisine as "South Asian" and frames it as a comfort craving
