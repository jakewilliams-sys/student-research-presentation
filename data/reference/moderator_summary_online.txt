Deliveroo Student & Young Adult Research
1. Delivery plays a "fallback" role in daily life
Delivery is not treated as a luxury, it is used when plans break down.
Primary triggers
No food in the house
Too tired to cook
Busy study/work days
Social evenings
Treat/reward moments
Delivery acts as a reliable safety net when energy and planning are low.
Implication:Deliveroo's strongest role is in moments of friction or fatigue, not planned meals.
2. Highly individual food behaviour
Even in shared households, food decisions are mostly individual:
Separate groceries
Separate cooking
Different schedules
Different diets
Group meal planning is rare. Delivery removes coordination effort and allows people to eat independently.
Implication:Solo ordering remains dominant behaviour.
3. Order thresholds and basket value expectations
There is clear evidence that minimum order thresholds and total basket requirements create friction for student users, particularly for solo takeaway orders and smaller grocery topups.
Participants frequently described:
Checking whether an order felt "worth it" once delivery fees and service charges were added
Adding extra items purely to reach minimum spend
Deciding not to order if they could not justify the total cost
Feeling that smaller, spontaneous orders were harder to justify
This was especially pronounced when ordering alone or ordering groceries for top ups rather than full shops. While thresholds did not prevent ordering altogether, they often acted as a point of hesitation and could reduce conversion in lower value occasions.
Students showed greater willingness to meet thresholds when:
Ordering in groups
Strong discounts were present
The order felt like a treat or reward
However, for everyday or convenience led moments, minimum spend requirements could feel high relative to student budgets.
Implication:There is an opportunity to explore more student friendly threshold mechanics (e.g. lower minimums, targeted offers, or perceived value incentives) to reduce friction on solo orders and grocery top ups without undermining basket value.
3. Deals and student value perception
One of the most consistent themes across sessions was the importance of deals, discounts and student specific value.
Students frequently:
Compare apps based on offers
Change restaurant choice based on discounts
Expect student specific pricing
Look for visible value before ordering
Promotions were described as a key driver of both:
App choice
Order frequency
Restaurant selection
Participants described switching orders when discounts appeared and actively looking for promotional offers before deciding where to order.
There was also a clear expectation that platforms should:
Recognise student status
Offer tailored deals
Provide visible savings
Some students explicitly suggested more personalised or student targeted deals as something that would make platforms feel more "for them".
Implication:Deals are not just a bonus they shape behaviour.Student relevant and personalised offers strongly influence choice and loyalty.
4. Frequency and usage patterns
Typical ordering patterns:
1–2x per week → heavy users
Every 1–2 weeks → moderate users
Occasional → fallback behaviour
Delivery is embedded in routines but used primarily for convenience and energy saving.
5. Key decision drivers
Most consistent ordering triggers:
Time pressure
Low energy after study/work
Empty fridge
Social occasions
Weekend treat behaviour
Convenience and mental effort reduction matter more than price alone.
6. Social media and discovery
Food discovery is heavily driven by:
TikTok → recipes & viral food
Instagram → local restaurants
Influencers
Implication:
Social drives consideration, but convenience drives conversion.
7. Grocery vs takeaway relationship
Users balance:
Groceries = planned & budget led
Delivery = reactive & convenience led
Many delivery orders happen because:
Ingredients are missing
Plans changed
Energy is low
This creates strong "top up" and hybrid ordering opportunities.
Rider & Flyer Insights
Rider perceptions
Riders are seen as central to the experience.
Positive themes
Friendly
Fast
Reliable
Key brand touchpoint
Good rider interactions reinforce trust and repeat usage.
Rider recruitment flyer feedback (undergrads)
The rider recruitment flyer tested performed positively overall, with students saying they would notice it and likely scan it.
What worked
Clear and minimal messagingStudents liked that the flyer was simple and direct.
"It doesn't have a lot going on… it's straight to the point."
Flexibility + earning resonatedThe ability to work around studies felt realistic.
"Study in the day and then do deliveries at night… it does make me think."
QR code drove engagementThe QR code was the strongest interaction driver.
"Anything with a QR code is appealing… I just want to scan it."
"I would definitely scan that."
Noticeability on campusStudents said they would spot it in university spaces.
"If you've seen this hanging up… I'd spot it."
Money messaging landed
"I'd be like, oh money, let's go."
Area for improvement: representation of students
A recurring piece of feedback was that the imagery did not strongly reflect real students.Some participants noted the people shown did not feel representative of typical student life, which slightly reduced relatability.
While this did not stop engagement with the flyer, making visuals feel more authentically "student" could improve identification and relevance.
Implication:Keep the minimal messaging and QR led design, but consider updating imagery to better reflect real student lifestyles and diversity.
Subscription perceptions 
Awareness
Awareness of subscription products was mixed.
Some students were aware of delivery subscriptions generally
Awareness of Deliveroo Silver/Roo Free specifically was lower or inconsistent
Uber One had slightly stronger recognition due to broader ecosystem use
Several participants only became aware of Deliveroo's student or subscription benefits during the session itself.
Implication:Discovery and clarity of Roo Free/Silver benefits appear limited among students.
What students like about subscriptions
Subscriptions are attractive when they clearly deliver visible, repeat value.
Students responded positively to:
Free delivery
Clear savings per order
Student specific pricing
Feeling they are "getting their money back"
Bundled benefits across multiple uses
Subscriptions feel worthwhile when:
They order regularly
The value is obvious
Savings are easy to understand
What they dislike or question
The main barriers were:
1. Uncertainty of valueStudents questioned whether they would order enough to justify the cost.
They often mentally calculate: "Will I actually use this enough?"
2. Budget sensitivitySubscriptions can feel like an extra commitment on top of:
Rent
Food
Bills
Study costs
3. Lack of perceived differentiationSome felt delivery subscriptions across platforms were similar, making them compare based on:
Price
Offers
Frequency of use
Deliveroo vs Uber One perception
Uber One often had stronger perceived value because:
It combines transport + food
Feels more widely used
Benefits are more visible
Participants noted that Uber's ecosystem can make its subscription feel broader in value, even if they still use Deliveroo for food.
Deliveroo was seen positively for:
Food delivery experience
Restaurant choice
Reliability
But subscription value needed to feel:
Clearer
More student specific
More obviously beneficial
Role of deals in subscription appeal
Subscriptions feel most worthwhile when paired with:
Discounts
Student pricing
Frequent promotions
Without strong visible savings, students question whether the subscription is worth keeping.
Implications for Deliveroo
1. Awareness gap existsMany students are not fully aware of Roo Free/Silver benefits or student relevance.
2. Value needs to be obvious and immediateStudents want to quickly understand:
What they save
How often
Why it's worth it
3. Student specific positioning mattersSubscriptions feel more compelling when framed explicitly for student life and budgets.
4. Uber One sets the comparison standardUber's broader ecosystem makes its subscription feel more valuable, even if actual savings are similar.
One line takeaway
Subscriptions are appealing to students when value is clear and frequent, but awareness and perceived differentiation of Deliveroo's student subscription offering is lower than competitors, particularly compared with Uber One.
Overall Takeaways for Deliveroo
1. Deliveroo is a convenience safety net, not just a treat platformStudents rely on delivery when energy, time or planning breaks down. It plays a practical role in busy study/work lives. Growth comes from owning these friction moments.
2. Deals and student specific value drive behaviour more than brand preferenceDiscounts significantly influence:
Which app is chosen
Which restaurant is selected
Whether an order happens at all
Students expect platforms to recognise their financial constraints. Personalised or student targeted deals would meaningfully strengthen loyalty and perceived relevance.
3. Minimum order thresholds create friction for solo and grocery occasionsStudents often hesitate when:
Fees stack up
They need to add items to hit minimum spend
The total doesn't feel "worth it"
Thresholds don't stop ordering entirely, but they reduce conversion in smaller, spontaneous or grocery top up moments. Student friendly mechanics could unlock incremental volume.
4. Subscription awareness and differentiation are opportunities Awareness of Roo Free/Silver was inconsistent. Students evaluate subscriptions based on:
Clear, visible savings
Frequency of use
Immediate value
Uber One often feels stronger due to ecosystem breadth (transport + food), even if usage behaviour differs. Deliveroo's subscription needs clearer communication of savings and stronger student positioning.
5. Ordering behaviour is highly individualisedEven in shared homes, most students:
Shop separately
Cook separately
Order separately
Delivery reduces coordination friction. Solo ordering remains the dominant behaviour pattern.
6. Riders are a key brand touchpointRider friendliness, speed and reliability strongly influence overall platform perception. Good rider experiences build trust and repeat behaviour.
7. Rider recruitment messaging can cut through on campusThe tested recruitment flyer performed well:
Clear and minimal messaging resonated
Flexibility + earning around studies landed strongly
QR code drove likely engagement
However, imagery could better reflect authentic student life
This suggests physical campus activation remains viable when creativity is sharp and student relevant.
Strategic Summary
Deliveroo is embedded as a convenience tool within student life.
The biggest growth levers are:
Stronger, more visible student deals
Reduced friction on smaller orders
Clearer subscription value communication
Continued reinforcement of reliability and rider quality
Students are value sensitive, convenience driven, and highly responsive to personalised incentives.
